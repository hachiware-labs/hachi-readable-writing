# Refrigerator: Repair or Replace?

Our current refrigerator is nine years old. It still cools, but it sometimes makes a loud operating noise. The immediate question is whether to spend ¥38,000 on a repair with uncertain remaining life, or replace it now with a ¥148,000 model.

The appliance savings fund has ¥220,000, but ¥80,000 from that fund is already needed for a school-related payment next month. That leaves ¥140,000 available for the refrigerator. A replacement would therefore leave the fund ¥8,000 short of that school payment. Because we will not borrow or use installment payments, replacing it now is not affordable from this fund alone.

Repairing it would cost ¥38,000 and comes with a 90-day warranty on the repaired part. After both the repair and the school payment, ¥102,000 would remain in the fund. The drawback is that we do not know how long the refrigerator would last after repair.

The replacement candidate includes delivery and removal for ¥148,000 and has a five-year warranty. The store estimates electricity savings of ¥1,500 per month, but this is not guaranteed. Even if that estimate proves accurate, the ¥110,000 difference between replacement and repair would take about 73 months to recover through electricity savings alone—longer than the five-year warranty.

For the current budget, the practical choice is to repair the refrigerator while it is still cooling. This keeps the school payment covered and preserves money in the appliance fund for a later replacement.

If the refrigerator stops cooling, replacement takes priority. In that situation, we will need to identify an additional ¥8,000 or another cash source before purchasing the ¥148,000 model, since the current fund cannot cover both the replacement and next month’s school payment.
