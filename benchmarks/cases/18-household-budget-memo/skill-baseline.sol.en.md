# Family Memo: Refrigerator Repair or Replacement

## Decision we need to make

The refrigerator still cools, but its intermittent loud noise may indicate a developing problem. We need to decide whether to spend less now on a repair with an uncertain useful life or replace it with a more expensive model that may lower electricity costs.

## Budget position

The appliance savings fund currently contains ¥220,000. Because the ¥80,000 school payment is due next month from the same fund, only ¥140,000 is available for the refrigerator without affecting that payment.

| Option | Refrigerator cost | Fund remaining after refrigerator and school payment |
|---|---:|---:|
| Repair | ¥38,000 | ¥102,000 |
| Replacement | ¥148,000 | −¥8,000 |

The replacement price includes delivery and removal, but it exceeds the amount available after reserving the school payment by ¥8,000. Since we will not borrow or use installment payments, we cannot choose this replacement from the fund alone unless we first add ¥8,000 or reduce the purchase price.

## Comparing the options

Repairing the refrigerator would preserve ¥102,000 in the fund after the school payment. However, the ¥38,000 quote includes only a 90-day warranty on the repaired part, and we do not know how long the nine-year-old refrigerator would continue working afterward.

The replacement would provide a five-year warranty and is estimated to save ¥1,500 per month in electricity. That saving is not guaranteed. Compared with repairing, replacement costs an additional ¥110,000. At the estimated electricity saving, recovering that difference would take about 74 months, or just over six years. This calculation does not account for future repairs or changes in electricity use.

## Proposed approach

Because the refrigerator is still cooling and the replacement would leave the fund ¥8,000 short of next month’s school payment, repairing it is the option that currently fits the household budget.

Before authorizing the repair, we should confirm what fault the quote covers and whether the technician believes the noise is likely to lead to loss of cooling. If we repair it, the remaining ¥102,000 should stay available for a future replacement rather than be treated as general savings.

If the refrigerator stops cooling, replacement takes priority as agreed. In that case, we will need to cover the ¥8,000 gap from new savings, another available source of cash, or a lower-priced refrigerator, while still avoiding borrowing and installment payments.

## Decision for the family

Unless the refrigerator stops cooling or the repair is judged unlikely to address the problem, proceed with the ¥38,000 repair and continue building the appliance fund toward replacement.
