### Kamakura, Despite the Rain

On June 14, we met at Kamakura Station at 9:00 a.m.—the three of us from university, together for the first time in ten years. We had planned to walk along the coast and visit two temples. It sounded like the perfect day trip.

Then the heavy rain began, almost as soon as we met.

To make things worse, one of us had worn new shoes and soon developed sore feet. We quickly gave up on the coastal walk and found a café near the station to wait for the rain to ease. At first, we were all disappointed. We had made the trip to Kamakura, but it seemed as though the day had already been ruined.

Gradually, though, the disappointment gave way to conversation. We started catching up properly and talked about things that had never come up in our messages: changing jobs, caring for a parent, and a child’s schooling. It felt strange and familiar at the same time, as if ten years had passed but our friendship had been waiting patiently for us.

We stayed in that same café until after noon. The server refilled our hot water several times, and the café became our shelter from the rain—and, in a way, from the busy lives we had all returned to. We had planned to see beautiful places, but the most meaningful part of the morning was simply sitting together with nowhere else to go.

The rain finally eased in the afternoon, so we visited one nearby temple instead of the two we had planned to see. The wet hydrangeas were beautiful, and the temple grounds were unusually quiet. It was not the Kamakura day we had imagined, but the quietness suited us. After the noisy disappointment of the morning, the temple felt like a small gift.

On the way home, we agreed that next time we should not over-plan. More importantly, we promised to meet again within a year, rather than waiting another decade.

We missed the coast and one of the temples, and I still wish we had been able to enjoy the day we planned. But the rain gave us time to talk in a way that messages never could. In the end, Kamakura did not go according to plan—but perhaps that was why we finally had the chance to catch up.
