We met at Kamakura Station at nine on June 14, three university friends together for the first time in ten years. We had planned a full day: a walk along the coast, then two temples. Instead, heavy rain began almost as soon as we said hello, and one pair of new shoes quickly turned into sore feet.

At first, it was hard not to feel disappointed. We had all made the trip, and Kamakura was right there beyond the rain, with the sea walk and temple paths we had imagined. We took shelter in a café near the station, telling ourselves we would wait only until the weather improved.

But the rain kept falling, and the conversation kept opening. Somewhere between the first cups and the server’s patient refills of hot water, we moved past the updates we had exchanged in messages. We talked about changing jobs, caring for a parent, and the complicated questions around a child’s schooling. Ten years had passed without us quite noticing how much life had filled in around the friendship we remembered.

We stayed in that café until after noon. When the rain finally eased, we managed to visit one nearby temple instead of two. It was quieter than it might have been on a clear day. The hydrangeas, darkened by rain, seemed brighter against the wet paths, and the stillness of the grounds felt like part of the day we had actually been given.

We missed some of the sights, and I still hope we get another chance to walk the coast. But I am glad the day did not go according to plan. If everything had gone smoothly, we might have spent more time moving from place to place than sitting long enough to hear one another.

Next time, let’s plan less tightly—and meet again before another ten years pass.
