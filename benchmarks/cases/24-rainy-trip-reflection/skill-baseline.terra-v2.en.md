We met at Kamakura Station at nine on June 14, three university friends together for the first time in ten years. We had planned to walk along the coast and visit two temples, but heavy rain began almost as soon as we found each other. Then one of us admitted that her new shoes were already making her feet sore.

After travelling all that way, it was hard not to feel disappointed. We took shelter in a café near the station, watching the rain blur the streets outside and wondering whether the day had already slipped away.

But as we waited for the weather to ease, the conversation changed. We began with the easy updates we had exchanged in messages, then moved into the things that had never quite fit there: changing jobs, caring for a parent, and worries about a child’s schooling. We stayed until after noon, and the server quietly refilled our hot water several times.

When the rain finally softened in the afternoon, we visited one nearby temple instead of two. The hydrangeas were wet and vivid, and the grounds were unusually quiet. We had missed much of what we came to see, and I still felt that small regret. But the slower day gave us something we had not planned for: time to hear each other properly.

On the way home, we agreed not to over-plan the next trip—and, more importantly, to meet again within a year.
