# Proposal: Two Evenings of Extended Library Hours

## Opening: The decision before us

Today I am asking the operations meeting to approve a three-month trial of extended library hours on Tuesdays and Thursdays, from June through August, together with a budget of ¥420,000 for additional security and utilities.

The proposal is intentionally limited. We cannot staff longer hours every weekday, but we have clear evidence that our current closing time prevents many residents from using the library. A two-evening trial will let us meet that need, measure the result carefully, and decide whether continued service is justified.

## Why later access matters

The library is currently open from 9:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m., Tuesday through Sunday. Those hours serve many users well, but they leave little room for people whose school or workday ends near closing time.

In March, we surveyed 320 library users. Sixty-two percent said they wanted weekday access after 7:00 p.m. The strongest demand came from office workers and high-school students—two groups most likely to be unavailable during daytime hours.

Our entrance data points in the same direction. During weekdays in April, we averaged 84 entries between 5:00 and 6:00 p.m. The daytime hourly average was 46. In other words, the hour immediately before closing drew almost twice the typical daytime traffic.

This does not prove that every one of those visitors would stay later. But it is strong evidence that demand is still active when we close our doors. A short, measured trial is the practical way to learn how much of that demand continues beyond 6:00 p.m.

## The proposed service

Under this proposal, the library would remain open until 8:00 p.m. on Tuesdays and Thursdays only.

The service after 6:00 p.m. would focus on the functions most likely to help evening users: first-floor reading space, borrowing, and returns. The children’s area and meeting rooms would close at the usual time of 6:00 p.m.

This narrower evening service is important. It gives office workers and students access to collections and study space while keeping the trial operationally realistic. We are not proposing to run the full building for two additional hours.

The trial would begin in June and end in August. This three-month period is long enough to observe recurring patterns, including different school and work schedules, without committing the library to a permanent change before we have results.

## Staffing and safety

Each late shift would include two library staff members and one security guard.

We do not have staffing capacity to extend hours every day, which is why the proposal is limited to two evenings per week. Existing shifts would be rearranged to cover the trial. Total overtime is expected to remain within 24 hours per month.

The added security presence is part of the operating model, not an afterthought. Evening access needs to be safe for users and staff, and the guard will support closing procedures as well as the security of the first-floor service area.

By closing the children’s area and meeting rooms at 6:00 p.m., we also reduce the space that must be staffed and monitored during the extended period. The smaller operating footprint makes the late service more manageable while preserving the services users are most likely to need.

## Cost and value

The additional security and utility costs are estimated at ¥420,000 for the full three-month trial.

That is the budget approval requested today. It is a defined amount for a defined period, rather than an open-ended operating commitment.

The question is not simply whether later hours have a cost. They do. The question is whether the cost produces enough public access to justify continuing. The trial is designed to answer that question with actual use, cost, safety, and workload data rather than assumptions.

## How we will evaluate the trial

During June through August, we will record:

- Visitors and loans by time of day
- Trial costs
- Staff workload
- Any safety issues

We will use those records to assess the service against three continuation conditions.

First, the library must average at least 50 visitors per evening after 6:00 p.m. This is a meaningful threshold: it would show that late hours are serving a substantial group rather than only a small number of occasional users.

Second, there must be no serious safety problems. The safety of visitors and staff is a condition of continuing the service, not a trade-off against attendance.

Third, costs must remain within budget. If the trial requires more resources than planned, we will need to review whether the service can continue in its proposed form.

These criteria give us a clear decision point at the end of August. If the trial meets them, we will have evidence for continuing or refining the service. If it does not, the trial will end without creating an unmanaged long-term commitment.

## What approval will accomplish

Approving this proposal will allow the library to respond to a demonstrated access problem for people who cannot reliably visit before 6:00 p.m.

It will give office workers a chance to borrow materials or use reading space after work. It will give high-school students a place to study and use library resources after their school day. And it will let us test the service in a controlled way that respects our staffing limits.

We are not being asked today to approve later hours indefinitely. We are being asked to approve a three-month experiment with a limited schedule, a defined operating model, a ¥420,000 budget, and clear conditions for continuation.

## Request

I recommend that the operations meeting approve:

- Extended library hours until 8:00 p.m. on Tuesdays and Thursdays
- A three-month trial from June through August
- A budget of ¥420,000 for additional security and utilities
- Evaluation against visitor use, safety, cost, and staff workload, with continuation dependent on at least 50 visitors per evening after 6:00 p.m., no serious safety problems, and costs remaining within budget

This is a measured response to clear user demand. It gives us the information needed to decide responsibly whether later library access should become part of our regular service.
