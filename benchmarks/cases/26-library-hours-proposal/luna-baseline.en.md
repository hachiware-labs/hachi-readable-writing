# Presentation Script: Proposal for Extended Library Hours

Good morning, everyone.

Today I am asking the operations meeting to approve a three-month trial that would keep the city library open until 8:00 p.m. on Tuesdays and Thursdays, from June through August, with a budget of ¥420,000.

Our current hours are 9:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m., Tuesday through Sunday. These hours serve many users well, but they do not meet the needs of people who are working or attending school during the day.

In March, we surveyed 320 library users. Sixty-two percent said they wanted weekday access after 7:00 p.m. The strongest demand came from office workers and high-school students—two groups who are often unable to visit before 6:00 p.m.

Our daily entry data also suggests that demand is already building in the late afternoon. On weekdays in April, an average of 84 people entered the library between 5:00 and 6:00 p.m. By comparison, the daytime hourly average was 46 people.

That means the final hour of our current schedule had almost twice the average daytime traffic. We cannot assume that everyone who wants to use the library after work or school will come during that one-hour period. Some users may be leaving just as they arrive because the library is about to close.

The proposal is therefore to test a limited extension: remain open until 8:00 p.m. on Tuesdays and Thursdays for three months.

This schedule is designed to respond to demand while recognizing our staffing limits. Staffing is not available for an extension every day, so a full-week change would not be practical at this time. Tuesdays and Thursdays provide two regular opportunities each week and allow us to test whether the demand is consistent across multiple evenings.

The trial would not mean that every library service remains available until 8:00 p.m. The children’s area and meeting rooms would close at 6:00 p.m. After 6:00, the available services would be limited to first-floor reading, borrowing, and returns.

This arrangement focuses the late opening on the services most likely to be useful to working adults and students. It also keeps the operating model simple and helps us manage staffing and safety.

The late shift would include two staff members and one security guard. Existing shifts would be rearranged, and total overtime is expected to remain within 24 hours per month.

This staffing plan gives us a reasonable level of service and ensures that staff are not working alone during the extended period. The security guard is also important because the building will remain open later than usual and because the trial will help us assess any safety concerns before considering continuation.

The direct additional security and utility costs for the three-month trial would total ¥420,000. I am asking the meeting to approve this amount as the trial budget.

This is a controlled investment. We are not committing to permanent extended hours today. We are asking for approval to collect evidence over three months and then make a decision based on actual results.

During the trial, we will record several types of information.

First, we will record the number of visitors after 6:00 p.m. and the times at which they enter.

Second, we will record loans and returns during the extended period. This will show us whether the late opening is being used primarily for reading, borrowing materials, returning items, or a combination of services.

Third, we will track costs, including security, utilities, and overtime.

Fourth, we will monitor staff workload. Staff feedback will be essential. We need to know whether the revised shifts are manageable, whether closing tasks are affected, and whether the service level can be maintained safely.

Finally, we will record any safety or operational problems. This includes incidents involving visitors, building access, equipment, or staff.

The purpose of this data collection is to answer a straightforward question: does opening later on two evenings provide enough public benefit to justify the additional cost and workload?

We have already established clear criteria for continuation.

The extended hours would need to attract at least 50 visitors per evening after 6:00 p.m. There must be no serious safety problems. And the costs must remain within the approved budget.

These criteria give us an objective basis for deciding what happens after August. If the trial meets all three conditions, we can consider continuing the schedule and returning with a further proposal. If it does not, we can end the extended hours or revise the model based on what we learned.

There are several benefits to conducting this trial.

The first is improved access. Office workers and high-school students are currently among the groups with the strongest demand, but many of them cannot use the library during normal weekday hours.

The second is better use of our existing facility. The data shows that the library is already busy in the hour before closing. Extending access may allow us to serve users at a time when they are actually available.

The third is that a limited trial reduces risk. Two evenings per week is enough to gather useful information without requiring a permanent staffing commitment or a daily increase in operating costs.

The fourth is accountability. We will not judge the program only by general impressions. We will measure attendance, use of services, costs, workload, and safety.

There are also reasonable concerns.

One concern is whether the number of visitors will justify the expense. That is why the 50-visitors-per-evening threshold is important.

Another concern is staff fatigue. The plan addresses this by rearranging existing shifts and limiting expected overtime to 24 hours per month.

A third concern is safety. The two-person late shift and one security guard are included specifically to support safe operation. We will also monitor incidents throughout the trial.

A fourth concern is that some users may expect access to all library facilities after 6:00 p.m. We should communicate the limited evening services clearly: first-floor reading, borrowing, and returns will remain available, while the children’s area and meeting rooms will close at 6:00 p.m.

To summarize, the evidence indicates meaningful demand for later access. Sixty-two percent of surveyed users requested weekday access after 7:00 p.m. Demand was strongest among office workers and high-school students. Late-afternoon traffic averaged 84 visitors per hour, compared with a daytime average of 46.

The proposal is modest and measurable: two evenings per week, until 8:00 p.m., for three months. It includes two staff members, one security guard, limited services, a budget of ¥420,000, and clear continuation criteria.

I therefore request that the operations meeting approve:

1. The three-month extended-hours trial from June through August.
2. Opening until 8:00 p.m. on Tuesdays and Thursdays.
3. The proposed late-shift staffing arrangement.
4. The ¥420,000 budget for additional security and utility costs.
5. The collection and review of attendance, service-use, cost, workload, and safety data.

This trial will give us a practical way to respond to community needs while protecting staff capacity and maintaining financial control.

Thank you. I ask for your approval to proceed.
