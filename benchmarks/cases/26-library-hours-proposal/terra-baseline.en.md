## Proposal: Extended Library Hours Trial

Good morning. I am asking for approval to run a three-month trial of later library hours on Tuesdays and Thursdays, from June through August, with a budget of ¥420,000.

Our library currently operates from 9:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m., Tuesday through Sunday. Those hours serve many residents well, but they exclude people whose work or school schedules make it difficult to arrive before closing. The question today is whether a limited, measured extension can serve that unmet need without creating an unsustainable staffing or cost burden.

The evidence suggests that there is a real demand.

In our March survey of 320 library users, 62 percent said they wanted weekday access after 7:00 p.m. That is a clear majority. The strongest interest came from office workers and high-school students—two groups for whom a 6:00 p.m. closing time is especially restrictive.

Our current visitor patterns reinforce that finding. During weekdays in April, an average of 84 people entered the library between 5:00 and 6:00 p.m. By comparison, the daytime hourly average was 46 people. In other words, the final open hour is already one of our busiest periods. This does not prove that every one of those visitors would stay later, but it is strong evidence that demand rises precisely when many residents finish work or school.

At the same time, we should be realistic about our capacity. Staffing is not available to extend hours every weekday. Rather than attempting a broad change that we may not be able to sustain, I recommend a focused trial: remain open until 8:00 p.m. on Tuesdays and Thursdays only.

This schedule gives the public two predictable evening options each week. It also allows us to test demand across the week while keeping the operational impact contained. Tuesday and Thursday are simple, easy-to-communicate days, and they give users time to form a routine around later access.

The proposed trial would run from June through August. A three-month period is long enough to capture more than initial curiosity. It will allow office workers, students, and other residents to learn about the new hours and decide whether they fit into their regular schedules. At the same time, the trial has a defined end point, so the operations team can evaluate actual results before making any continuing commitment.

The late shift would include two staff members and one security guard. We would rearrange existing shifts to cover the extension, while keeping total overtime within an expected maximum of 24 hours per month.

The service model after 6:00 p.m. would be deliberately limited. The children’s area and meeting rooms would close at the normal time. Evening visitors would have access to first-floor reading, borrowing, and returns. This approach concentrates staff and security where they are needed, keeps the late service straightforward, and preserves the core functions that evening users are most likely to need.

The additional security and utility cost for the full three-month trial is estimated at ¥420,000. That is the budget approval requested today. Because the trial uses rearranged shifts rather than adding a full new staffing pattern, the proposal keeps costs focused on the direct requirements of operating safely after 6:00 p.m.

It is important that this not become an open-ended experiment. We will collect evidence throughout the trial: visitor counts and loans by time of day, operating cost, and staff workload. These measures will tell us not only whether people are coming in the evening, but also whether they are using library services and whether the operational model is manageable.

Continuation after August would require three conditions.

First, we would need an average of at least 50 visitors per evening after 6:00 p.m. This is a meaningful threshold: it would show that the extended hours are serving a substantial number of people rather than only a small number of occasional users.

Second, there must be no serious safety problems. The presence of a security guard is intended to support that condition, but safety performance must be judged by what actually occurs during the trial.

Third, costs must remain within budget. The ¥420,000 allocation gives us a clear financial boundary, and the monitoring process will show whether the service can be delivered within it.

This proposal is modest in scale, but it addresses a real access gap. A resident who finishes work at 6:00 p.m. currently has little practical opportunity to use the library on a weekday. A high-school student may face the same problem after classes, clubs, and travel home. Opening until 8:00 p.m. twice a week gives those residents a realistic chance to read, borrow materials, and return items without changing the library’s full operating model.

The trial also protects us from making a permanent decision based only on survey responses or assumptions. We already have evidence of demand, but we need operational evidence as well: who comes, when they come, what they use, what it costs, and what the work requires of staff.

For those reasons, I recommend that the operations meeting approve the June-through-August trial of extended hours until 8:00 p.m. on Tuesdays and Thursdays, along with the ¥420,000 budget for additional security and utilities.

At the end of the trial, we will return with the results and a recommendation based on the agreed criteria: average evening attendance, safety, staff workload, and cost.

Thank you.
