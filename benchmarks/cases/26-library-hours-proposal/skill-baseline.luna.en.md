# Extending Library Hours: A Three-Month Trial

## Slide 1 — The proposal

Good afternoon.

Today I am asking the meeting to approve a three-month trial that would keep the city library open until 8:00 p.m. on Tuesdays and Thursdays, from June through August.

The request is for approval of both the trial and its ¥420,000 budget.

The proposal responds to a clear pattern: many residents need access after work or school, but our staffing capacity does not support extending hours every day. A focused trial on two evenings a week would let us test the demand, manage the cost, and determine whether later opening hours should continue.

## Slide 2 — Why later access is needed

The library is currently open from 9:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m., Tuesday through Sunday. That schedule serves daytime users well, but it limits access for people whose work or school day ends in the late afternoon.

In the March survey of 320 library users, 62 percent said they wanted weekday access after 7:00 p.m. The strongest demand came from office workers and high-school students.

This is important because these groups are likely to be unavailable during much of our current opening period. For them, the question is not whether the library is useful. The question is whether they can reach it before it closes.

## Slide 3 — What current usage tells us

April entry data shows that demand is already increasing near closing time.

On weekdays, an average of 84 people entered the library between 5:00 and 6:00 p.m. The average number entering during a daytime hour was 46.

That means the hour immediately before closing was significantly busier than a typical daytime hour. We should be cautious about assuming that everyone arriving before 6:00 p.m. would stay later, but the pattern suggests that closing time is occurring while demand is still strong.

Together, the survey and entry data indicate a practical opportunity: later access is wanted, and current evening demand may be sufficient to justify testing it.

## Slide 4 — A targeted two-evening trial

Because staffing is not available for an extension every day, the proposal concentrates the trial on Tuesdays and Thursdays.

On those two days, the library would remain open until 8:00 p.m. The trial would run for three months, from June through August.

This schedule gives office workers and students two predictable opportunities each week. It also creates a manageable operating pattern for staff. Tuesdays and Thursdays are frequent enough to generate meaningful data, while limiting the additional cost and workload compared with a daily extension.

This is not a proposal to change the library’s full service model immediately. It is a controlled test of whether two later evenings provide enough public value to continue.

## Slide 5 — What will be available after 6:00 p.m.

The late opening would provide a focused set of services.

After 6:00 p.m., the first-floor reading area would remain available. Visitors could also borrow materials and return items.

The children’s area and meeting rooms would close at 6:00 p.m. These areas require additional supervision and have different staffing needs, so keeping them open is outside the scope of this trial.

This limited service model allows us to offer the most important evening functions—quiet study, reading, borrowing, and returns—while keeping the late shift practical and predictable.

Visitors would therefore receive meaningful extended access, but they would also encounter clear boundaries about which spaces and services remain available.

## Slide 6 — Staffing, safety, and cost

Each late shift would include two staff members and one security guard.

Existing shifts would be rearranged to support the trial. Total overtime is expected to remain within 24 hours per month. This keeps the staffing plan within a defined limit and avoids assuming that additional personnel are available for every evening.

The additional security and utility costs for the full three-month trial would total ¥420,000.

The security guard is included because the library would be open after its usual closing time, with fewer staff and fewer areas in use. The two-staff arrangement is intended to maintain basic service coverage and ensure that staff are not working alone during the late period.

## Slide 7 — How we will judge the trial

During the trial, we will record four kinds of information: visitor numbers by time, loans by time, operating cost, and staff workload.

These measures will help us distinguish between simple attendance and sustained use of library services. We will be able to see how many people arrive after 6:00 p.m., how many borrow materials, what the trial costs, and how the revised shifts affect staff.

Continuation would require three conditions.

First, the library must receive at least 50 visitors per evening after 6:00 p.m. Second, there must be no serious safety problems. Third, the costs must remain within the approved budget.

These conditions give us a clear basis for a later decision. The trial would not continue automatically just because it has begun.

## Slide 8 — Benefits and limits

The main benefit is improved access for people who cannot reliably use the library before 6:00 p.m. Two later evenings may also help distribute visits more evenly across the week and make the library more useful as a place for reading and study.

The trial also has limits. It will not provide late access every weekday. The children’s area and meeting rooms will not be available after 6:00 p.m. And three months may not capture every seasonal pattern in library use.

For those reasons, the trial should be understood as a decision-making exercise as well as a service improvement. It will show whether a limited schedule can meet enough demand to justify the added cost and workload.

## Slide 9 — The decision requested

I am asking the meeting to approve two things today.

First, approve the June-through-August trial, with the library open until 8:00 p.m. on Tuesdays and Thursdays.

Second, approve the ¥420,000 budget for the additional security and utility costs.

The operating conditions would be those described today: two staff members and one security guard on each late shift, rearranged existing shifts, overtime expected to remain within 24 hours per month, and first-floor reading, borrowing, and returns available after 6:00 p.m.

At the end of the trial, we will review the visitor, loan, cost, workload, and safety data against the continuation requirements.

## Slide 10 — Closing

The evidence points to a focused opportunity.

A majority of surveyed users want weekday access after 7:00 p.m. Demand is strongest among office workers and high-school students, and weekday use is already highest during the hour before closing. At the same time, staffing limits make a daily extension unrealistic.

A two-evening trial gives us a practical middle ground. It expands access where the need appears strongest, keeps the service scope manageable, sets a defined budget, and establishes measurable conditions for continuation.

I recommend that we approve the trial and the ¥420,000 budget.
