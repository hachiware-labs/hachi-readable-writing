## Operations Meeting Presentation Script  
**Proposal: Three-Month Trial of Extended Library Hours**  
**Estimated speaking time: 9–10 minutes**

Good morning, everyone.

Today, I am asking the operations meeting to approve a three-month trial of extended evening hours at the city library, together with a budget of ¥420,000.

The proposal is straightforward: from June through August, the library would remain open until 8:00 p.m. on Tuesdays and Thursdays. Our current opening hours—9:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m., Tuesday through Sunday—would remain unchanged on all other days.

This would be a limited, measurable trial. It would allow us to respond to clear user demand while keeping the operational and financial risks under control.

First, let me explain why we are proposing this change.

Our current closing time makes weekday access difficult for people who work standard office hours and for high-school students who have classes or after-school activities. By the time these users can reach the library, they may have only a short period before closing—or may not be able to visit at all.

The March user survey gives us strong evidence that this is a real access problem. Of the 320 users surveyed, 62 percent said they wanted access on weekdays after 7:00 p.m. Demand was especially strong among office workers and high-school students.

The April visitor figures support the survey results. On weekdays, an average of 84 people entered the library between 5:00 and 6:00 p.m. By comparison, the daytime hourly average was 46 visitors.

In other words, the final hour of the weekday schedule attracted about 83 percent more visitors than an average daytime hour. That suggests demand is still high when we close at 6:00 p.m. It is reasonable to test whether a significant number of users would continue to use the library between 6:00 and 8:00.

At the same time, we know that extending the hours every weekday is not currently practical. We do not have enough staff to support a daily extension, and we should not commit to a major permanent change before measuring actual evening use.

For that reason, we are proposing two evenings per week: Tuesdays and Thursdays.

These days would provide useful access at two points during the working week without creating consecutive late shifts. They would also give us enough operating days to identify patterns in attendance, borrowing, costs, and staff workload.

The trial would run for three months, from June through August. A three-month period is long enough to gather meaningful data and observe whether interest is sustained after the initial publicity. It is also short enough to limit our financial exposure if demand does not meet expectations.

Let me outline how the extended hours would operate.

On Tuesdays and Thursdays, the library would remain open until 8:00 p.m. The late shift would consist of two library staff members and one security guard.

We would rearrange existing staff shifts rather than simply adding overtime to the current schedule. Total overtime is expected to remain within 24 hours per month. We would monitor those hours closely throughout the trial to ensure the arrangement remains manageable and does not create an unreasonable burden for staff.

After 6:00 p.m., the library would operate with reduced services. The children’s area and meeting rooms would close at the normal time of 6:00. Only the first floor would remain open.

Evening users would be able to use the first-floor reading area, borrow materials, and return items. Restricting operations to the first floor gives users access to the library’s core services while keeping staffing, supervision, lighting, and security requirements at a practical level.

We will make the reduced-service arrangement clear in all publicity and signs. Users should understand in advance that the extended hours are intended primarily for reading, borrowing, and returns, rather than full access to every library facility.

The additional security and utility costs for the entire three-month trial are estimated at ¥420,000. This is the budget for which we are seeking approval today.

That figure covers the added security presence and the utilities required to keep the first floor operating during the extra hours. Staff overtime will be managed within the proposed limit through shift rearrangement.

This is not a request to approve permanent extended hours. It is a request to invest ¥420,000 in a controlled trial that will give us reliable evidence for a longer-term decision.

Measurement is therefore a central part of the proposal.

During every extended evening, we will record the number of visitors entering after 6:00 p.m. We will also record loans by time, the costs of operating the service, and the effect on staff workload.

We should be able to answer several practical questions by the end of August.

How many people actually use the library after 6:00? Does attendance remain stable across the three months? How much borrowing takes place during the extended period? Are Tuesdays and Thursdays used equally? Can the late shift be operated safely and without excessive pressure on staff? And does the service remain within the approved budget?

We will use three clear conditions when deciding whether extended hours should continue.

First, the library must receive an average of at least 50 visitors per extended evening after 6:00 p.m.

Second, there must be no serious safety problems.

Third, the trial must remain within its approved budget.

These conditions give us an objective basis for evaluation. If the trial meets all three, we will have evidence to support discussing continuation. If it does not, the extended hours will end after August unless a revised proposal is separately approved.

There are, of course, operational risks to consider.

One concern is that evening attendance may be lower than the survey suggests. A survey measures interest, but only a trial can measure actual behavior. That uncertainty is precisely why we are proposing a temporary program with a clear attendance threshold.

A second concern is staff workload. We will address this through shift rearrangement, the two-person late staffing model, and monthly monitoring of overtime. Keeping the children’s area and meeting rooms closed will also reduce the number and complexity of duties after 6:00.

A third concern is safety. A security guard will be present throughout the late period, and public access will be limited to the first floor. Any incidents will be documented and reviewed immediately. A serious safety problem would be grounds for reconsidering or ending the trial.

Finally, there is the question of cost. The proposed ¥420,000 is a defined, three-month amount—not an open-ended commitment. We will track actual costs during the trial and will not recommend continuation unless operations remain within budget.

Overall, this proposal balances access with operational caution.

The survey tells us that users want later weekday access. The visitor figures tell us that demand is especially high just before the current closing time. Office workers and high-school students—the groups that have the greatest difficulty reaching the library during existing hours—would gain a realistic opportunity to use its services.

At the same time, the proposal recognizes our staffing limits. It extends hours only two days per week, uses a reduced-service model, limits evening access to the first floor, provides dedicated security, and establishes clear measures of success.

To conclude, I am asking the meeting to approve two items:

First, a three-month extended-hours trial from June through August, with the library remaining open until 8:00 p.m. on Tuesdays and Thursdays.

Second, a budget of ¥420,000 for the additional security and utility costs.

At the end of the trial, we will report the results to the meeting, including attendance, loans, actual costs, safety incidents, and staff workload. Any proposal to continue the extended hours will be based on those results and will require a separate decision.

This is a modest and carefully controlled way to test whether our operating hours can better match the needs of the community.

I recommend that the meeting approve the trial and its ¥420,000 budget.

Thank you. I welcome your questions.
