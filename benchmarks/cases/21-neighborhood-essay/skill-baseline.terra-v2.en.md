Six months after I moved to Morioka, I still sometimes wake before I remember where I am.

For fifteen years, I lived in Nagoya. I knew its rhythms without thinking: the crowded platforms, the humid summer evenings, the convenience stores that seemed to appear exactly when I needed one. I knew which streets became noisy after work, where to meet a friend without giving detailed directions, and how the city changed when rain threatened. Nagoya had become less a place I noticed than the background against which my days unfolded.

In February, I left it because of my spouse’s job transfer. I continued working remotely, which meant that while my spouse entered a new workplace and met new colleagues, I spent most weekdays at home with my laptop. The move had an odd quality of being both sudden and slow. Boxes arrived, furniture found its place, and our new address became familiar on forms. Yet I felt as though some part of me had remained in Nagoya, waiting for the rest of me to return.

Morioka announced itself first through snow.

I had seen snow before, of course, but I had never had to organize ordinary life around it. In the first weeks, I watched people walk confidently along icy streets while I moved as if the pavement might betray me at any moment. I bought shoes with better grip and still stared down at my feet. A short trip to the supermarket felt like an expedition. I worried about falling, about getting lost, about not knowing the unspoken rules everyone else seemed to understand.

There was also the dialect. In shops or on the street, I sometimes caught every individual word but not the shape of the conversation. At other times, a familiar phrase arrived with a different sound and left me wondering whether I had understood it correctly. People were kind, but kindness did not immediately make me feel at ease. I answered carefully, often too carefully, afraid of seeming rude or foolish. Outside of necessary errands, I rarely went anywhere.

From the window of our home, I could see Mount Iwate.

Each morning, before opening my computer, I looked for it. Some days it stood plainly against the sky, its broad outline seeming close enough to measure. Other days it was partly hidden by haze, and sometimes it disappeared altogether behind clouds. At first, looking at the mountain was simply a habit I acquired because it was there. It marked the beginning of the day. It gave the view beyond the neighboring buildings a depth I had not expected.

I did not think of it as a relationship. I was still an observer: someone newly arrived, looking out.

That began to change in April, when my neighbor, Sasaki, stopped me in the hallway. She had seen me coming and going with grocery bags, she said, and asked whether I might like to help tend the neighborhood flower beds sometime. I hesitated. Gardening was not something I knew much about, and the thought of making conversation with people I had never met made me nervous. But I also knew that if I said no, I might continue watching Morioka only from behind the glass.

So, on a Saturday morning, I went.

There were several people already gathered near the flower beds, wearing work gloves and jackets that had seen many springs. Someone handed me a small shovel. Someone else pointed to bags of soil. For the first few minutes, I concentrated so hard on being useful that I barely spoke. I worried that I would put the wrong soil in the wrong place, or stand where I was in the way. The conversation moved around me in a mixture of practical instructions, jokes, and local expressions that I could not always follow.

Then one of the others asked where I had moved from. When I said Nagoya, the group responded with the kind of interest people have when a faraway place becomes briefly present. They asked about the heat, the food, and whether I missed the city. I did miss it, I told them. Then I admitted that I was still nervous about the snow.

That opened a different kind of conversation. While we carried soil and loosened the ground, people told me which nearby shops were especially useful, when to stock up before heavy weather, how to tell whether a side street might be icy even after the main roads had been cleared. These were small pieces of knowledge, offered without ceremony. Yet they were exactly what I had lacked. They made the city feel less like a landscape I had to decode alone.

Since then, I have joined the flower-bed work about twice a month. The tasks are ordinary: pulling weeds, watering, trimming back what has grown too freely, clearing away what has finished blooming. The work has not made me suddenly outgoing. I still sometimes pause before speaking, and I still miss parts of the dialect. But I have learned that familiarity does not always arrive through a dramatic welcome or a decisive moment of confidence. Sometimes it grows through repeated, modest acts: recognizing someone’s gloves, being asked whether I have tried a particular bakery, noticing that the same corner catches the afternoon sun.

The flower beds have changed, too. In April, they were mostly dark soil and possibility. Now they hold colors I can recognize from the road. I know which plants were placed where, which ones needed extra attention, and which ones surprised us by thriving. Passing them on my way to the store, I feel a small, quiet satisfaction. I have no special claim on them, but I have helped care for them.

In June, I returned to Nagoya for a business trip. The city felt immediately familiar. I knew how to move through the station, how to read the pace of the streets, how the air seemed to gather around you in the early summer heat. I met colleagues, visited places I had once passed every day, and felt the ease of being somewhere that had long required no explanation.

But in the hotel room at night, I found myself missing Morioka’s mornings.

I missed opening the curtains and looking for Mount Iwate. I missed the quieter streets and the cooler air. I missed the sense that my day began with a view large enough to interrupt my thoughts. It surprised me, not because Nagoya had become less important, but because Morioka had become present in me even while I was away from it.

Now, when clouds hide Mount Iwate, I feel slightly disappointed. That feeling is small, but it tells me something. The mountain has become part of my ordinary attention, like a familiar face glimpsed at the start of the day. I still do not know every street in Morioka. I still walk carefully when winter’s memory returns to the pavement. I still have much to learn.

I do not want to say that my hometown has changed. Nagoya remains one of the places that made me who I am. But over these six months, another place has begun to make room for me. Not all at once, and not completely. It has happened through snow, a window, a neighbor’s invitation, shared soil, and mornings when the mountain is clear.

I am not replacing one home with another. I am gradually learning that belonging can expand.
