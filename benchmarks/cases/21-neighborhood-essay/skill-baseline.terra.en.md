## Another Place to Belong

Six months after moving to Morioka, I still sometimes wake up with the faint expectation that I will hear the sounds of Nagoya outside the window.

For fifteen years, Nagoya was simply where life happened. I knew its streets well enough to move through them without thinking. I knew which stations made me hurry, which cafés were good for waiting out the rain, and which routes home felt reassuring after a long day. Even the ordinary friction of the city had become familiar: the crowds, the traffic, the sense that something was always in motion.

Then, in February, my spouse’s job transfer brought us to Morioka. I continued working remotely, which meant that while my spouse entered a new workplace and met new colleagues, I spent much of each day alone at home, facing a city I did not yet know.

The first weeks were quieter than I had expected. Snow covered the sidewalks, and I was afraid of falling on the icy roads. I watched other people walk steadily past our building in shoes that seemed to know what to do, while I took small, cautious steps and looked down at the ground. Going out for anything beyond shopping felt like a task that required preparation. I worried about the weather, about getting lost, and about not understanding people when they spoke.

The local dialect was especially humbling. I could catch parts of conversations, but sometimes familiar Japanese seemed to move in an unfamiliar rhythm. A phrase would arrive with warmth and speed, and I would smile a moment too late, hoping that my expression had answered appropriately. No one was unkind. Still, I felt like a guest who had entered a room after the conversation had already begun.

During those first months, Mount Iwate became the one part of Morioka that I knew without effort. I could see it from our window every morning. Some days it appeared sharply against the sky, with snow still visible on its slopes. On other days it was pale and distant, almost blending into the clouds. I did not have to speak to it or understand it. I only had to look.

At first, looking at the mountain was a kind of routine. It marked the beginning of the day: make coffee, open the curtains, check the weather, see whether Mount Iwate was there. Gradually, though, it became something more like a quiet companion. In a place where so much still felt uncertain, the mountain gave shape to the mornings.

In April, my neighbor Sasaki invited me to help tend the neighborhood flower beds. I almost declined. I imagined having to make conversation while doing work I did not know how to do. I worried that I would misunderstand instructions or stand awkwardly at the edge of the group. But Sasaki asked in such a natural way that refusing seemed stranger than joining. “We’ll be out there for a while,” Sasaki said. “Come if you like.”

On the first day, I did not know what to say. I carried bags of soil and tried to follow what everyone else was doing. The work gave my hands something useful to do, but my mind remained busy. Was I placing the soil in the right spot? Should I offer to do more? Was there a moment when I was supposed to speak?

Then, little by little, people began talking to me.

It happened without any formal introduction. Someone pointed out a nearby shop that sold good vegetables. Someone else explained which streets were easiest to walk when the snow became heavy. A person I had only seen from a distance told me where to find warm winter boots and how residents deal with icy mornings. The advice was practical, but it also carried something else: an assumption that I would still be here when the next winter came.

That feeling stayed with me.

I had thought of myself as someone temporarily unsettled, someone who had arrived because of circumstances and would need time to decide whether the city could become familiar. But while we carried soil together, the people around me spoke as if I had already become part of the neighborhood’s future. They did not ask me to prove that I belonged. They simply told me what I might need.

Since then, I have joined the flower-bed work about twice a month. I still do not always understand every word of the dialect, and I am still not the most talkative person there. But now I recognize voices before I see faces. I know which person will mention a new shop, who will notice when the soil is dry, and who has a particular way of talking about the coming season. I have learned that conversation does not always begin with an interesting story. Sometimes it begins with carrying a bag of soil beside someone.

In June, I returned to Nagoya for a business trip. The city felt immediately familiar. I moved through stations without checking signs as carefully as I do in Morioka. The rhythm of the streets, the density of people, even the humid air seemed to belong to a version of myself I had known for a long time.

I was glad to be there. Nagoya remains deeply familiar to me, and I felt the comfort of returning to a place where I had spent fifteen years. Yet I was surprised by what I missed.

I missed Morioka’s quiet mornings.

In Nagoya, I found myself thinking of the view from our window. I wondered whether Mount Iwate was visible that day. I missed the slower beginning of the morning, before work messages and errands had gathered their momentum. It was not that Nagoya had become less important. Rather, Morioka had become present enough in my life that its absence could be felt.

When I returned, I looked for the mountain the next morning almost immediately. The sky was cloudy, and Mount Iwate was hidden. To my surprise, I felt slightly disappointed.

That small disappointment told me more than any grand declaration could have. I do not think a hometown changes all at once. Nagoya is still a place that holds years of memory, habit, and affection. I do not want to pretend that six months in a new city can replace fifteen years in another.

But perhaps belonging does not have to mean replacement. It may be possible to carry one place with you while slowly making room for another. A mountain seen from a window, a neighbor’s invitation, the weight of soil in your hands, a conversation you only partly understand—these things can gather quietly. Before you notice, they begin to form a life.

I am still learning Morioka: how to walk through winter, how to listen more carefully, how to recognize the changing seasons in the flower beds. But when clouds cover Mount Iwate now, I find myself waiting for it to appear again.

That is not yet a declaration that this is my hometown. It is something gentler, and perhaps more honest. I am beginning to have another place where I belong.
