# Another Place to Belong

Every morning, before I begin work, I look out the window at Mount Iwate.

In February, when I moved to Morioka, I did not know that the mountain would become part of my daily routine. I had lived in Nagoya for fifteen years. It was the city I knew how to move through without thinking: the roads, the stations, the shops, the pace of people around me. When my spouse was transferred for work, we moved north, while I continued working remotely.

The decision made sense on paper. My work would remain the same, and Morioka was simply where our life would now be based. But a place does not become familiar because an address changes. At first, Morioka felt like a landscape I had entered without learning its rules.

Winter made that especially clear. I was afraid of walking on snowy roads. Even a short trip required more attention than I was used to giving it. I watched where I placed my feet, worried about slipping, and returned home feeling that I had accomplished something more demanding than buying groceries. I rarely went out except to shop.

The local dialect added another layer of uncertainty. I could understand the general meaning of what people said, but not always the feeling behind their words. Sometimes I wondered whether I had missed a joke or misunderstood an invitation. In Nagoya, I had been able to participate in conversations easily, even when I was tired or distracted. In Morioka, I listened carefully, translating not only words but also pauses and expressions.

Most of my days passed inside. I worked at my desk, looked out the window, and watched the light change over the snow. Mount Iwate was there almost every morning. Its presence was steady, but at first it belonged more to the view than to me. I could see it, but I did not yet feel connected to the place it represented.

Spring arrived gradually. In April, my neighbor, Sasaki, invited me to help tend the neighborhood flower beds. I had not expected the invitation, and I was unsure how to respond. I did not know Sasaki well. I did not know who else would be there or what I would be expected to do. Most of all, I did not know what to say.

Still, I went.

On the first day, I concentrated on carrying soil. It was a task I could understand without explanation. I lifted, carried, and emptied the bags where I was shown. While we worked, people began telling me about nearby shops and the practical ways they lived through winter. Their advice was simple, but it was also an introduction. They were showing me the neighborhood as something more than streets and buildings. They were giving me the knowledge that comes from living somewhere for a long time.

I learned which shops were nearby and how people prepared for the cold months. I learned that local knowledge is rarely offered as a formal lesson. It appears in conversation, in a passing suggestion, or in the answer to a question you did not know you needed to ask. The flower beds became a place where I could listen without having to perform familiarity.

Since then, I have joined the group about twice a month.

The work itself changes with the season, but the feeling of arriving has become familiar. I no longer wonder as much about where to stand or what to do first. Sometimes I contribute to the conversation; sometimes I mostly listen. Neither feels like a failure now. I have begun to understand that belonging does not always announce itself through a moment of complete ease. It can begin with returning to the same place, recognizing the same faces, and carrying soil beside people who were strangers a few months earlier.

My work is still remote, and there are days when I spend most of them indoors. Morioka has not transformed every part of my life. I still notice the distance between what I understand and what I do not. I still occasionally need people to repeat themselves. The fear I felt on snowy roads has not disappeared entirely, either. But it has become smaller, mixed with experience.

In June, I returned to Nagoya on a business trip. The city was immediately familiar. I knew how to walk through it, where to look, and how to fill the waiting time between one place and another. For fifteen years, that familiarity had seemed like the definition of home.

Yet during the trip, I found myself missing Morioka’s quiet mornings.

It surprised me. I had expected to feel only relief at being back in a place I knew so well. Instead, I noticed the absence of the mountain. I missed looking out the window before starting work. I missed the stillness that had once felt empty and now seemed to make room for thought.

When I returned to Morioka, I began to notice something else. I felt slightly disappointed when clouds hid Mount Iwate. The disappointment was not dramatic. I did not feel that the day had been ruined. I simply became aware that I had been expecting to see it.

That expectation told me more than I could have explained at the time. The mountain had moved from the background of my life into its rhythm. It was no longer just a landmark I had been told to recognize. It had become part of what I looked for, part of how I measured the beginning of a day.

Six months after moving, I am careful about what I call home. I do not want to declare that my hometown has changed. Nagoya remains the place where I spent fifteen years, where familiarity was built slowly and almost invisibly. A business trip can still return me to it within minutes. I can walk its streets with the confidence of memory.

What has changed is not necessarily Nagoya, but the shape of my own belonging.

I now feel that I am gradually gaining another place where I belong. That feeling is still unfinished. It does not erase loneliness or uncertainty, and it does not require me to stop missing the city I left. It is made from quieter things: a mountain seen from the window, a conversation held while carrying soil, advice about winter, and the decision to show up again twice a month.

Perhaps belonging is not a door that opens all at once. Perhaps it is a path that becomes visible because we keep walking it. In February, I was afraid to cross the snow-covered streets. By April, I was carrying soil with my neighbors. By June, I was away from Morioka and missing its mornings.

Now, when the clouds lift and Mount Iwate appears, I notice it with a feeling that is both ordinary and new. I am not certain when Morioka became part of my life. I only know that, little by little, it did.
