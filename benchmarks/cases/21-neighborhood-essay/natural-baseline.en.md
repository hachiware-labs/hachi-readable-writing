# Another Place to Belong

This February, I moved from Nagoya to Morioka because of my spouse’s job transfer. I had lived in Nagoya for fifteen years—long enough for the city to become the background of my life. I knew how long familiar journeys would take, which shops carried what I needed, and how the streets changed from one season to the next. When someone asked where I lived, I could answer without feeling that any explanation was necessary.

My work did not change with the move. I continued working remotely, sitting in front of the same computer and speaking with many of the same people. On the screen, my days looked almost as they had before. Yet when I stepped away from my desk, everything was different.

The first difference was the snow.

I had seen snow before, of course, but seeing it and living with it were not the same thing. At first, even walking along a snowy road frightened me. I watched where I put each foot and moved so carefully that short trips seemed to take forever. Going outside felt like a small expedition. Because I worked at home, I had no daily commute forcing me to become accustomed to the streets. For the first several weeks, I rarely went out except to buy groceries and other necessities.

Language, too, sometimes made me feel like an outsider. I would hear the local dialect in shops or brief exchanges nearby and fail to catch what had been said. Often I understood the general meaning only a moment too late. I became more hesitant to begin conversations, worried that I would ask someone to repeat themselves or respond in the wrong way.

During those early months, Mount Iwate was my most dependable connection to Morioka.

I could see it from the window, and every morning I looked toward it. Some days it appeared sharp and clear; on others it seemed softer and more distant. I did not know enough about the mountain to feel that it belonged to me in any way. Still, its presence gave shape to the place where I had arrived. Before I learned the streets or knew many people, I knew where to look for Mount Iwate.

Then, in April, my neighbor Sasaki invited me to help tend the neighborhood flower beds.

I almost declined. I was not sure whether I would be useful, and I worried about standing among people who already knew one another while having nothing to say. But I also knew that waiting until I felt completely comfortable might mean waiting forever, so I went.

On that first day, my fears seemed justified. I did not know how to enter the conversation. Everyone else appeared to understand what needed to be done, while I waited for instructions and tried not to get in the way. But once we began carrying soil, the silence no longer felt quite so important. There was work to share. Someone showed me where to put the soil. Someone else pointed out what would be planted.

As we worked, people began telling me about the neighborhood. They recommended nearby shops and explained small practical things about daily life. The conversation eventually turned to winter: what to prepare, what to watch out for, and how people here manage when the snow returns. None of it was presented as a formal lesson. The advice came in pieces between lifting, carrying, and arranging the flower beds.

I had spent much of my first winter thinking of snow as something I had to confront alone. That morning, I began to understand that winter here was also a body of knowledge passed from one person to another. People knew how to live with it, and they were willing to teach me.

Since then, I have joined the flower-bed work about twice a month. I still do not always know what to say, and I cannot claim that I have suddenly become part of every conversation. Belonging, I am learning, does not necessarily arrive as a strong feeling. Sometimes it begins with being expected at a certain time, recognizing a few faces, or knowing which task to pick up without being told.

In June, I returned to Nagoya for a business trip. As soon as I arrived, the city felt familiar. My body seemed to remember its pace. I did not have to think about how to move through it, and the ease of that feeling was comforting.

Yet while I was there, I found myself missing Morioka’s quiet mornings.

That surprised me. Until then, I had thought of Morioka mainly as the place I was still learning to live in and Nagoya as the place I understood. But apparently, without my noticing, Morioka had begun to enter my sense of an ordinary day. Its quiet was no longer emptiness. It was something I had grown used to, and something I wanted to return to.

Six months after the move, I still look at Mount Iwate every morning. The gesture is the same as it was in February, but the feeling has changed. Now, when clouds hide the mountain, I am slightly disappointed. It is a small disappointment, but perhaps that is why it matters. We are disappointed by the absence of things we have come to expect and care about.

I am not ready to declare that my hometown has changed. Fifteen years in Nagoya cannot be replaced by six months elsewhere, nor do I think it needs to be. Nagoya remains a place of familiarity, memory, and ease.

But Morioka is no longer simply the city to which I moved because of my spouse’s job. It is the mountain outside the window, the flower beds that need tending, and the people who taught me about shops and winter while we carried soil together.

Rather than losing one hometown and choosing another, I feel that I am gradually gaining an additional place where I belong. The change is quiet, like the mornings I missed when I was away. I may not always be able to see it clearly, but it is there—much like Mount Iwate behind the clouds.
