# Another Place to Belong

Six months ago, I moved to Morioka from Nagoya, where I had lived for fifteen years. The move was not the result of a long-held dream or a carefully imagined new beginning. My spouse was transferred for work, and I followed, continuing my own job remotely. In practical terms, the arrangement was simple: the work would stay the same, while the place around me changed.

In reality, everything changed.

We arrived in February, when winter still had a firm grip on the city. In Nagoya, snow had always felt like an event—something to watch through a window, photograph, and talk about. In Morioka, snow was part of the road, the sidewalk, the roof, and the daily schedule. I was afraid of walking. I worried about slipping, about getting in someone’s way, about not knowing what to do when the snow began to melt and refreeze. Even a short trip to the supermarket required more concentration than I expected.

The cold made the city seem larger. So did the dialect. I could understand the words people spoke, at least in theory, but not always their rhythm or meaning. Sometimes I nodded because I thought I had understood, only to realize later that I had missed something important. I was embarrassed to ask people to repeat themselves. I became quiet.

For the first few weeks, I rarely went anywhere except to shop. I worked at home during the day and watched the changing light from the window. Every morning, Mount Iwate appeared in the distance. Some days it was sharply outlined against the sky, its broad shape seeming almost close enough to touch. On other days it was pale and indistinct, as if it were still deciding whether to show itself.

I began to look for it without thinking. Before opening my computer, before making coffee, I would glance out the window. The mountain became the first thing I checked each morning. It did not speak to me, of course, and it did not make any promises. Still, its presence gave the unfamiliar days a kind of order. I was in Morioka, and Mount Iwate was there.

At first, I thought that watching the mountain was enough. It gave me something to notice while I remained apart from the life of the neighborhood. But simply observing a place is different from living in it. I knew this, although I did not yet know how to cross the distance between the two.

In April, my neighbor, Sasaki, invited me to help tend the flower beds in the neighborhood. The invitation was casual. There was no ceremony to it, and perhaps that made it easier to accept. I said yes before I had time to think of reasons not to.

On the first day, I had no idea what to say. I worried that I would not know anyone’s name, that I would misunderstand an instruction, or that my unfamiliarity would be obvious. I wondered whether I should talk about myself, ask questions, or simply keep working. In the end, I mostly carried soil.

There is something helpful about carrying soil. It gives the hands a task and leaves the mind free to listen. As we worked, people told me about nearby shops, roads that became difficult in winter, and small ways of managing daily life in Morioka. They explained which places were convenient, which stores had good vegetables, and how they prepared for the coldest months. Their advice was practical, but it also felt like an introduction—not to a tourist’s Morioka, but to the city as it is lived in.

I learned that conversation does not always begin with the right words. Sometimes it begins with showing up and doing the work in front of you.

Since then, I have joined the group about twice a month. The flower beds have changed gradually, almost too slowly to notice from one visit to the next. Small plants have become larger. Bare spaces have filled with color. The work itself is modest: removing weeds, turning soil, watering, and carrying things from one place to another. Yet those hours have begun to mark my time here.

I have also changed in ways that are difficult to measure. I am less afraid of the roads. I ask people to repeat themselves when I do not understand. I recognize more faces, and some people recognize me first. I have places I can recommend now, even if my recommendations are still tentative. I no longer feel that every outing is an expedition.

In June, I returned to Nagoya for a business trip. After fifteen years there, the city was immediately familiar. I knew how to move through the stations, where to buy a quick meal, and how long it would take to walk between places. The streets seemed to remember me, or perhaps I remembered them so well that I no longer had to pay attention.

But during the trip, I found myself missing Morioka. I missed the quieter mornings. I missed the particular quality of the air before the day became busy. I missed looking out the window and seeing Mount Iwate. Nagoya was still familiar, but familiarity was no longer the same thing as belonging.

Now, when clouds hide the mountain, I feel slightly disappointed. This surprises me. I used to think of the mountain as scenery, something beautiful but separate from my life. Somehow, without my noticing, it has become part of my morning. Its absence leaves a small blank space.

I am careful about what I call this feeling. I do not want to declare that Nagoya has stopped being my hometown, or that Morioka has replaced it. Fifteen years cannot be erased by a move, and belonging is not a competition between places. Nagoya holds my memories, my routines, and many versions of the person I have been. Morioka is becoming something different: a place connected to the person I am still becoming.

Perhaps moving does not always mean leaving one home behind and finding another. Sometimes it means making room for one more place. A mountain viewed from a window. A neighbor named Sasaki. Soil carried in both hands. Conversations that began before I knew how to join them.

I have lived in Morioka for only six months. There is still much I do not understand, and winter will return with all the lessons I have yet to learn. But I am no longer only looking at this city from the outside.

Little by little, Morioka is becoming another place where I belong.
