# When the Mountain Is Hidden

Six months ago, I moved from Nagoya to Morioka. I had lived in Nagoya for fifteen years, long enough for the city to become the background against which I measured ordinary life. I knew how long familiar journeys would take and where to find what I needed. Even the noise belonged to a pattern I understood.

We moved in February because of my spouse’s job transfer. My own work continued remotely, so although my address changed, my working days did not. Each morning I sat down in front of the same computer and spoke with many of the same people as before. That continuity should have made the move easier. Instead, it sometimes made Morioka feel like a place just outside my life: visible whenever I looked up from work, but not yet connected to me.

Snow covered the roads when we arrived. I was afraid of walking on them. Every step seemed to require attention, and I watched other people move with an ease I could not imitate. Conversations were another source of uncertainty. I often struggled to understand the local dialect, catching part of what someone said and trying to guess the rest from tone and expression. I became hesitant about starting conversations because I was afraid that I would not know how to answer.

For the first couple of months, I rarely went out except to shop. Working from home made that easy. I could finish a day without speaking face-to-face with anyone besides my spouse. The new city remained a collection of practical facts: the way to the supermarket, the condition of the roads, the forecast for the next morning.

Yet every morning, I looked through the window at Mount Iwate.

At first, the mountain was simply the most striking part of the view. Its presence made the distance between Nagoya and Morioka feel real. I did not yet know the neighborhoods around me, but I could look toward the mountain and recognize where I was. It gave the morning a shape. Before checking messages or beginning work, I would notice whether the summit was clear or covered by clouds.

In April, my neighbor Sasaki invited me to help tend the neighborhood flower beds. I agreed, although I was nervous. By then the roads were easier to walk, but I still felt unsure among people who already knew one another. They shared a history of small encounters and familiar routines from which I was absent. On the first day, I did not know what to say.

The work helped. There was soil to carry, and carrying it gave me a reason to stand beside someone without having to invent a conversation. As we worked, people told me about nearby shops. They also talked about how to live through the winter. The subject was practical, but it meant more to me than a list of useful advice. Winter had been the season in which I first encountered Morioka as an outsider, watching every step and retreating indoors whenever I could. Hearing other people talk about it made the season seem less like a private test I had failed and more like something the whole neighborhood experienced together.

I did not suddenly become talkative that day. I still missed words, and there were moments when I was unsure whether I had understood correctly. But conversation no longer felt like something I had to produce on my own. It arose from the work: a bag that needed moving, a patch of ground that needed attention, a question about where something should go. We could talk while looking at the flower beds instead of directly at one another, and that made it easier.

Since then, I have joined the group about twice a month. It is not a dramatic commitment, and I am still new. I do not know everyone well, nor do I understand everything I hear. But the neighborhood has acquired voices. The streets are no longer only routes to the shops. They are also places where I may recognize someone, exchange a greeting, or remember a conversation held while carrying soil.

In June, I returned to Nagoya for a business trip. As soon as I arrived, the city felt familiar. I did not need to think about how to move through it. After fifteen years, familiarity was stored in my body as much as in my memory. There was comfort in that ease, and I was glad to be back.

What surprised me was that, while I was there, I missed Morioka’s quiet mornings.

Until then, I had thought of Nagoya as the familiar place and Morioka as the place I was still learning. That remained true, but it was no longer the whole truth. Missing Morioka did not mean that Nagoya had become less important. It meant that some part of my daily life had already taken root in the new city without my noticing. The quiet, the morning window, the mountain, and the neighborhood work had begun to form a pattern of their own.

Now, when I look outside and clouds hide Mount Iwate, I feel slightly disappointed. Six months ago, I could not have imagined caring whether the mountain was visible. It was beautiful, but it was separate from me. Now its absence changes the morning. I have come to expect it, much as I have come to expect the flower-bed gatherings twice a month and the possibility of meeting someone I know while I am out.

I am not ready to declare that my hometown has changed. A hometown is too complicated to transfer from one place to another after only six months. Nagoya holds fifteen years of my life, and familiarity of that depth does not disappear because I have moved away.

What I feel is quieter than that. Morioka is no longer simply the city to which my spouse’s job brought us, or the snowy place where I continued my old work from a new room. Through repeated mornings and ordinary work shared with neighbors, it is becoming somewhere I participate in, however modestly.

Perhaps belonging begins before we have a confident name for it. It begins with noticing what we miss when we leave, whose advice we remember, and what we hope to see when we open the curtains. I still belong to the place I came from. Slowly, without replacing it, I am gaining another place where I belong.
