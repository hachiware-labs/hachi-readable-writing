# Reviewing AI-Generated Meeting Summaries  
### 15-minute internal study session script

## 0:00–1:30 — Opening

Hello, everyone.

Today we’ll look at how to review meeting summaries created by AI.

Many of us have started using AI to summarize transcripts from online meetings. This can save time and help us organize discussions quickly. But an AI-generated summary should be treated as a draft—not as an automatically reliable record.

The purpose of this session is not to discourage AI use. It is to show how we can use it safely and confidently by applying a focused human review.

By the end of the session, you should know:

- What kinds of errors to expect
- Which parts of a summary require particular attention
- Who is responsible for checking it
- How to show readers that the review has taken place

---

## 1:30–4:00 — What we found in the trial

We reviewed 50 AI-generated summaries from last month’s trial.

Six contained errors in important facts, such as dates, amounts, or the person responsible for an action.

Four attributed a statement to the wrong speaker.

Five presented unresolved matters as if they had already been decided.

A single summary could contain more than one type of problem, so these figures should not be added together to calculate the number of affected summaries.

The most important lesson was that the errors were often difficult to notice. The prose sounded fluent and natural. Sentences were clear, professional, and plausible—even when their content was wrong.

That is one of the main risks of AI-generated text. Poorly written text makes us cautious. Smoothly written text can make us lower our guard.

So when reviewing a summary, we should ask not only, “Does this read well?” but also, “Is this actually supported by what was said?”

---

## 4:00–6:30 — How meaning can change

Let’s look at an example.

The AI-generated summary said:

> “We will introduce it next month.”

That sounds like a clear decision with a firm schedule.

But the actual remark was:

> “We will consider whether a trial can begin next month.”

Several important details disappeared.

First, “consider whether” became “will.” An open question became a commitment.

Second, “a trial” became a full introduction.

Third, “can begin next month” became a confirmed deadline.

The AI did not produce an obviously strange sentence. It produced a shorter, more decisive sentence. That is exactly why the problem could easily be missed.

When reviewing summaries, pay close attention to words that express uncertainty or conditions, such as:

- “may”
- “if”
- “subject to”
- “proposed”
- “under consideration”
- “trial”
- “tentative”
- “after approval”

Removing one of these expressions can completely change the meaning.

We also need to look for objections and conditions that may have been omitted. A summary might capture the preferred proposal while leaving out concerns about cost, timing, security, staffing, or approval. The result can appear accurate at sentence level while giving a misleading picture of the discussion as a whole.

---

## 6:30–10:30 — A practical review method

You do not need to verify every sentence with equal intensity. Focus first on information that could lead someone to act.

A useful checklist is: decisions, deadlines, owners, numbers, and open issues.

### Decisions

Confirm that every recorded decision was actually agreed.

Look for phrases in the original remarks such as “we agreed,” “approved,” or “we will proceed.” Do not assume that a proposal, preference, or suggestion became a decision simply because nobody immediately objected.

### Deadlines

Check every date and time period.

Confirm whether a date is final, proposed, approximate, or conditional. “By the end of May,” “around the end of May,” and “subject to approval, by the end of May” do not mean the same thing.

### Owners

Check who is responsible for each action.

AI can confuse the person who proposed an action, the person who discussed it, and the person who accepted responsibility for it. Verify both the person’s name and what they actually agreed to do.

### Numbers

Check amounts, quantities, percentages, and other figures.

A small transcription or summarization error can have a large practical impact. Pay attention to units as well: thousands versus millions, monthly versus annual figures, and totals versus per-person amounts.

### Open issues

Make sure unresolved questions remain clearly marked as unresolved.

Expressions such as “to be confirmed,” “under consideration,” or “pending approval” are valuable. Do not remove them merely to make the summary shorter or cleaner.

Finally, check for important objections and conditions. Ask:

- Was there a concern that affects the decision?
- Was the proposal conditional on approval or further investigation?
- Did someone present a significant alternative?
- Is there a risk that readers will think there was more agreement than there really was?

When you find a high-impact item, compare it with the relevant original remarks. Read enough surrounding context to understand whether the speaker was proposing, questioning, agreeing, or deciding.

---

## 10:30–12:00 — The transcript is not perfect either

There is one more complication: the source transcript can contain speech-recognition errors.

This means we should not always treat the transcript as unquestionable truth. Names, technical terms, numbers, and similar-sounding words are especially vulnerable.

If a transcript passage seems inconsistent or unclear:

- Listen to the relevant part of the recording, if available and permitted
- Check meeting materials or other reliable context
- Ask the speaker or another participant when necessary
- Avoid turning an unclear remark into a definite statement

The review is therefore not simply “summary versus transcript.” It is a check against the best available evidence of what was actually said and agreed.

Recordings and transcripts must continue to be handled according to our existing internal information-management rules. Using AI does not replace or relax those requirements.

---

## 12:00–13:30 — Responsibility and traceability

Before an AI-generated summary is shared with meeting participants, the person responsible for the meeting reviews it.

This establishes a clear point of accountability. The reviewer does not need to guarantee that every sentence is a perfect transcript. They do need to check the important facts and ensure that the document does not misrepresent decisions, responsibilities, or unresolved matters.

The document should also record:

- The reviewer’s name
- The review date

This allows readers to see that a human review took place. It also prevents a polished AI draft from being mistaken for an approved meeting record.

If the summary has not yet been reviewed, it should remain clearly identified as a draft and should not be shared as the confirmed outcome of the meeting.

---

## 13:30–15:00 — Closing

Let’s finish with four points.

First, fluent writing is not evidence of factual accuracy. AI-generated summaries can sound natural while changing important meaning.

Second, focus your review on decisions, deadlines, owners, numbers, and open issues. Also check for omitted objections and conditions.

Third, remember that transcripts can contain speech-recognition errors. When something important is unclear, verify it against the recording or another reliable source.

Fourth, the person responsible for the meeting reviews the summary before sharing it, and the document records the reviewer’s name and review date.

AI can reduce the time required to create a useful first draft. Human review turns that draft into something colleagues can safely rely on.

The practical rule is simple:

> Use AI to draft. Use the source to verify. Use a named reviewer before sharing.

Thank you.
