# Reviewing AI-Generated Meeting Summaries

## Slide 1 — A Fluent Summary Is Still a Draft

Today we will look at how to review meeting summaries created by AI.

Many of us have recently started using AI to summarize transcripts from online meetings. This can save time and make meeting records easier to prepare. However, an AI-generated summary should not be treated as a finished record simply because it reads naturally.

That is the central message of this session: use AI to create the draft, but verify the draft before anyone relies on it.

We are not trying to discourage AI use. We want to make it safer and more useful by focusing human attention on the details that matter most.

## Slide 2 — What We Found in the Trial

We reviewed 50 summaries from last month’s trial.

Six contained errors in important facts, including dates, amounts, or the people responsible for an action. Four attributed statements to the wrong speaker. Five presented unresolved matters as if the participants had reached a decision.

These categories overlap, so the numbers should not be added together to calculate the number of affected summaries. A single summary could contain more than one type of problem.

The important point is that these were not merely stylistic differences. A wrong deadline can cause work to be late. A wrong amount can affect planning or approval. Assigning an action to the wrong owner can create confusion about responsibility. Recording an open question as a decision can send a team in a direction it never agreed to take.

## Slide 3 — Why the Errors Are Easy to Miss

The summaries generally sounded natural. Their sentences were clear, and their structure looked like an ordinary meeting record. That fluency made the errors difficult to notice.

When we see awkward or broken text, we instinctively become cautious. When the prose is smooth and confident, we are more likely to accept it without checking. But natural language is not evidence that the content is accurate.

There is another complication: the source transcript may also contain speech-recognition errors. The AI may be summarizing a mistaken transcription rather than the words that were actually spoken. Names, numbers, technical terms, and similar-sounding expressions are especially vulnerable to this kind of error.

Reviewing a summary therefore requires more than proofreading. We are not only checking whether the writing is clear. We are checking whether the record accurately represents the meeting.

## Slide 4 — Meaning Can Change in a Small Rewrite

Consider this example from the review.

The generated summary said:

> “We will introduce it next month.”

The actual remark was:

> “We will consider whether a trial can begin next month.”

The generated sentence is shorter and easier to read, but it changes the meaning in several ways.

“Will introduce” turns a possibility into a commitment. “Next month” becomes a confirmed schedule rather than a proposed time. The words “consider whether” disappear, and so does the condition that the next step would be a trial.

This is exactly the kind of error that fluent prose can conceal. Every individual word sounds reasonable in the context of a business meeting, yet the final sentence records a decision that was never made.

When reviewing a summary, ask not only, “Does this sound plausible?” Ask, “What level of certainty did the speaker actually express?” A proposal, an intention, a condition, and a confirmed decision are not interchangeable.

## Slide 5 — What to Compare with the Original Remarks

Reviewers should compare the summary with the original remarks, concentrating first on information that could change someone’s actions.

Check each recorded decision. Confirm that the participants actually reached it and that the summary has not converted a suggestion or unresolved discussion into an agreement.

Check deadlines and other dates. Verify both the date itself and its meaning. A target date, an estimate, and a firm deadline are different.

Check owners. Confirm who accepted responsibility for each action. Do not assume that the person who raised a subject is also the person assigned to handle it.

Check numbers, including amounts and quantities. These details are easy to mishear or rewrite incorrectly, and even a small change can have significant consequences.

Finally, check open issues. Questions that remain unresolved should still appear unresolved in the summary. Readers need to know what requires further discussion rather than being given a false sense of closure.

These checks are not separate from understanding the meeting. Together, they answer the practical questions that readers bring to a meeting record: What did we decide? Who will act? By when? Based on which figures? And what is still open?

## Slide 6 — Look for What the Summary Left Out

Accuracy is not only about incorrect statements. A summary can contain no obviously false sentence and still misrepresent the meeting by omitting something important.

In particular, check whether the summary removed objections or conditions that affect a decision.

Suppose a proposal received general support, but one participant said it could proceed only after a security review. A summary that records the support but omits the condition makes the proposal appear ready to proceed. Similarly, leaving out a substantial objection can make agreement seem stronger than it was.

AI summaries often compress discussion to produce a clean result. That is useful when the omitted material is repetition or conversational noise. It is dangerous when the omitted material changes the scope, certainty, or acceptability of an action.

During review, look at the discussion surrounding each important conclusion. Ask whether the summary preserves the conditions, reservations, and objections that readers need in order to interpret it correctly.

## Slide 7 — Use a Focused Review Sequence

A practical review can follow a short sequence.

First, read the generated summary once to understand the structure and identify every claimed decision, action, deadline, owner, number, and open issue.

Second, locate the corresponding remarks in the transcript. Compare the meaning, not just matching words. Pay close attention to expressions such as “will,” “may,” “propose,” “consider,” and “subject to,” because they indicate different levels of commitment.

Third, check the relevant part of the recording when the transcript appears unclear or suspicious. Because speech recognition can be wrong, the transcript is useful evidence but not automatically the final authority.

Fourth, look around each important remark for an objection, condition, or qualification that the summary may have omitted.

Finally, correct the summary and read it again as a participant would. Confirm that it clearly separates decisions from proposals and completed agreements from open matters.

The purpose is not to reconstruct every sentence of the meeting. It is to verify the information that people may use to make decisions or take action.

## Slide 8 — Responsibility and Visible Review

The person responsible for the meeting reviews the summary before it is shared with participants.

This responsibility matters because that person is best placed to understand the meeting’s context and recognize when a concise sentence has changed the original meaning. AI can prepare the draft, but responsibility for the shared record remains with the person reviewing it.

The document should record the reviewer’s name and the review date. This does not guarantee that the summary is perfect. It gives readers a clear indication that someone checked it and shows when that review took place.

If the document has not yet been reviewed, it should not be presented to participants as an authoritative meeting record.

## Slide 9 — Existing Information Rules Still Apply

Using AI does not replace our existing internal information-management rules.

Recordings and transcripts may contain confidential or sensitive information. The same rules that already govern how those materials are handled continue to apply when AI is used to produce a summary.

Review quality and information management address different risks. Reviewing protects readers from inaccurate or misleading content. Following the existing rules protects the underlying meeting information. Safe use requires both.

## Slide 10 — The Working Habit to Keep

AI-generated summaries can reduce the effort needed to organize a meeting record, but their fluent language can create more confidence than their accuracy justifies.

Treat the output as a draft. Before sharing it, compare decisions, deadlines, owners, numbers, and open issues with the original remarks. Check whether objections or conditions have disappeared. When the transcript is doubtful, refer back to the recording. Then record the reviewer’s name and review date.

The goal is not to distrust every sentence or stop using AI. It is to place trust in the right process: AI creates a useful first draft, and a responsible person verifies the facts and meaning before others rely on it.
