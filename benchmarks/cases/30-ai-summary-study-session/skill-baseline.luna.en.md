# Reviewing AI-Generated Meeting Summaries

## Opening: A natural-sounding summary can still be wrong

Most of us have started using AI to make routine work easier. One common use is summarizing the transcript of an online meeting.

This is useful. A long transcript can become a short document that helps people remember what happened and what they need to do next.

But a summary is not reliable simply because it sounds professional.

Last month, we reviewed 50 meeting summaries from our trial. Six contained errors in important facts, such as dates, amounts, or owners. Four attributed statements to the wrong speaker. Five presented unresolved matters as decisions. Some summaries had more than one of these problems.

The wording was often natural and easy to read. That was part of the risk. When a sentence sounds clear and confident, we may accept it without checking whether it accurately reflects the meeting.

Today’s goal is not to discourage AI use. The goal is to use AI safely by treating its output as a draft that requires review.

## Why meeting summaries are especially sensitive

A meeting summary does more than describe a conversation. People may use it to decide what to do, when to do it, and who is responsible.

A small change in wording can therefore change the practical meaning of the meeting.

For example, one summary stated:

> “We will introduce it next month.”

However, the actual remark was:

> “We will consider whether a trial can begin next month.”

These sentences are not equivalent. The first describes a decision and a planned introduction. The second describes an unresolved possibility. It still requires consideration, and even the start of a trial has not been confirmed.

If this summary were shared without review, readers could begin preparing for an introduction that nobody had approved.

There is another source of uncertainty: the transcript itself may contain speech-recognition errors. AI is summarizing the transcript it receives. If a name, number, or sentence was transcribed incorrectly, the summary may preserve or worsen that error.

So there are two points to remember. The summary can misrepresent the transcript, and the transcript can misrepresent what was said. Reviewing the summary means checking the important meaning against the original remarks, not merely checking whether the summary is grammatically correct.

## What to check first

When reviewing a summary, start with the information that can change someone’s actions.

Check five areas:

- decisions;
- deadlines and dates;
- owners or responsible people;
- numbers and amounts;
- open issues.

These are the parts most likely to create immediate confusion if they are wrong.

For each decision, ask: Did the meeting actually reach a decision, or did participants only discuss an option?

For each deadline, ask: Was this date agreed, proposed, or mentioned as a possibility?

For each owner, ask: Did that person accept responsibility, or did someone merely suggest that they might handle the task?

For each number, ask: Does the amount, quantity, or estimate match the original remark?

For each open issue, ask: Is the summary showing that the matter remains unresolved?

This distinction between a decision and a possibility is particularly important. Meeting conversations often contain phrases such as “we could,” “we might,” “let’s consider,” or “we need to confirm.” These phrases indicate discussion or a condition, not necessarily approval.

A summary should preserve that level of certainty.

## Check who said what

The second review is about attribution.

A meeting summary may combine several people’s comments into one paragraph. In doing so, it can assign a statement to the wrong speaker or make it unclear who made a proposal, raised an objection, or accepted an action.

This matters because the speaker’s role can change the meaning.

A manager confirming a deadline is different from a participant suggesting one. A customer raising a concern is different from a project member agreeing that the concern has been resolved. Someone describing a risk is not necessarily accepting responsibility for addressing it.

When a speaker is named, compare the attribution with the transcript. If the speaker is not important to the meaning, neutral wording may be safer than a confident but incorrect attribution.

For example, instead of saying “Mika approved the change,” the summary may need to say, “The group discussed the change, but approval was not recorded,” if that is what the remarks support.

The reviewer does not need to verify every sentence in the transcript equally. The priority is to verify who made important proposals, objections, commitments, and decisions.

## Check what may be missing

Reviewing a summary is not only a search for incorrect statements. It is also a search for important omissions.

A summary can be technically accurate while still misleading if it leaves out an objection or a condition.

Suppose one participant says, “I support this approach if the security review is completed first.” If the summary records only “The participant supported the approach,” it has removed the condition. The remaining sentence suggests stronger agreement than the original remark.

Look for comments that limit or qualify a conclusion:

- objections;
- risks;
- dependencies;
- conditions for approval;
- requests for confirmation;
- questions that remain unanswered.

These points may be less prominent than the final discussion, but they can be essential to understanding what the group actually agreed to do.

A useful question is: If someone read only this summary, could they make a different decision from the one they would make after hearing the meeting?

If the answer is yes, the summary needs revision.

## A practical review method

The person responsible for the meeting reviews the summary before it is shared with participants. That person is usually best placed to recognize the context, the participants, and the intended meaning of the discussion.

The review can follow a simple sequence.

First, read the summary once to understand its overall account of the meeting. Notice what it presents as the main decisions, actions, and unresolved matters.

Second, return to the original remarks for each important item. Compare the summary with the relevant part of the transcript or recording. Confirm the wording, speaker, date, amount, owner, and degree of certainty.

Third, check the transcript or recording when the wording is unclear or when the transcript may contain a speech-recognition error. Pay particular attention to names, numbers, dates, and short phrases that could change the meaning.

Fourth, look for omitted objections and conditions. Ask whether the summary makes an unresolved issue appear settled or makes a conditional agreement appear unconditional.

Finally, revise the summary so that it reflects what is known and what remains open. If the meeting did not reach a decision, the summary should say that the matter remains under discussion or requires confirmation.

This is not a demand to compare every word. It is a focused check of the information readers will rely on.

## What a reviewed summary should communicate

A reviewed summary should make its status visible.

The document records the reviewer’s name and the review date so readers can see that it was checked. This does not guarantee that the summary is perfect, but it tells readers that someone with responsibility for the meeting examined its important content.

The summary should also distinguish among different kinds of statements. A confirmed decision, a proposed action, an assigned task, and an unresolved question should not all look the same.

Clear wording helps:

- “The team agreed to…” indicates a confirmed decision.
- “The team proposed…” indicates a proposal.
- “The group will confirm…” indicates that a follow-up is still needed.
- “The discussion remains open regarding…” indicates an unresolved issue.
- “Subject to the security review…” preserves a condition.

The exact wording will depend on the meeting, but the principle is consistent: the summary should show the level of certainty supported by the original remarks.

## Using AI responsibly

AI can reduce the effort required to produce a first draft. It can help identify topics, possible action items, and sections of a long discussion that deserve attention.

Its role is to support the review process, not to replace responsibility for the meeting record.

The reviewer remains responsible for deciding whether the summary is accurate enough to share. If an important point cannot be confirmed from the transcript or recording, it should not be presented as certain. The uncertainty should remain visible, or the point should be removed until it can be confirmed.

Existing internal information-management rules also continue to apply to recordings and transcripts. Using AI does not change how those materials should be handled. The same rules for access, storage, sharing, and retention still govern the source material and the resulting summary.

## Closing: Use the summary as a draft, not as evidence by itself

The main lesson is simple: fluent language is not proof of accuracy.

Our trial showed that errors can affect facts, speaker attribution, and the difference between a decision and an unresolved issue. Because a summary may contain more than one problem, checking one category does not complete the review.

Before sharing an AI-generated meeting summary, compare its important claims with the original remarks. Confirm decisions, deadlines, owners, numbers, and open issues. Check who said what, and make sure important objections and conditions have not disappeared.

Then record the reviewer’s name and the review date.

AI can remain a useful part of daily work. We make that use safer when we treat its output as a draft, preserve the uncertainty that existed in the meeting, and make a responsible person accountable for the final version.
