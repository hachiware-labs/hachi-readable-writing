# Reviewing AI-Generated Meeting Summaries Safely

## 1. Why this matters

AI has become a useful part of daily work, especially for turning long online-meeting transcripts into readable summaries. It saves time and gives us a practical starting point.

But a summary is not a record simply because it reads smoothly. Its natural prose can make small but important errors easy to miss. Today’s goal is not to discourage AI use. It is to show how we can use its output safely: as a draft that a responsible person reviews before sharing.

## 2. What we found in last month’s trial

We reviewed 50 AI-generated meeting summaries from last month’s trial.

Six summaries contained errors in important facts, such as dates, amounts, or assigned owners. Four attributed statements to the wrong speaker. Five described unresolved matters as though they had already been decided.

These categories can overlap, so they do not represent separate summaries. What matters is that plausible writing did not reliably mean accurate writing.

## 3. The risk is not only obvious mistakes

A clearly nonsensical sentence is easy to catch. The more difficult problem is a sentence that sounds professional but changes the meaning of the discussion.

For example, one summary stated: “We will introduce it next month.”

The original remark was: “We will consider whether a trial can begin next month.”

The first sentence describes a settled commitment. The original describes a future consideration about a possible trial. It changes the decision, the scope, and the certainty.

That difference could affect planning, expectations, and accountability.

## 4. Start with the right mental model

Treat the AI summary as a draft written by an assistant who was present only through the transcript.

The assistant may misunderstand context, combine separate remarks, or miss a qualification. It may also inherit errors from speech recognition in the source transcript.

So our review is not just proofreading. We are checking whether the summary faithfully represents what was said, what was decided, and what remains open.

## 5. What to compare with the original remarks

When reviewing a summary, compare the high-impact items with the original transcript or recording.

Check:

- Decisions: Was something actually agreed, or was it still under discussion?
- Deadlines: Is the date correct, and is it a commitment rather than a target or possibility?
- Owners: Is the responsible person correct and clearly identified?
- Numbers: Are amounts, quantities, and budgets accurate?
- Open issues: Are unresolved questions still presented as unresolved?

These are the details readers are most likely to act on. They deserve direct comparison rather than a quick read for general plausibility.

## 6. Check what may be missing

Accuracy also means preserving important limits on a statement.

Look for objections, conditions, and exceptions that could change how the meeting’s outcome should be understood. For example, a proposal may have received general support but remained conditional on budget approval, legal review, or a later test.

If the summary leaves out that condition, readers may assume the work can proceed immediately. The words may still be fluent, but the meeting’s meaning has changed.

## 7. A practical review sequence

The person responsible for the meeting reviews the summary before it is shared with participants.

A simple sequence is:

1. Read the summary once to understand its overall account.
2. Verify decisions, deadlines, owners, and numbers against the original remarks.
3. Confirm that open issues, objections, and conditions have not been omitted or turned into decisions.
4. Correct the summary where needed, or ask for clarification if the original discussion itself is unclear.
5. Share the reviewed version.

This approach focuses attention where an error is most likely to create follow-on work or confusion.

## 8. Make the review visible

The reviewed document records the reviewer’s name and the review date.

This is not a claim that the summary is perfect. It gives readers useful context: someone responsible for the meeting has checked it before distribution.

It also makes the handoff clear. AI can prepare the draft, but accountability for the shared meeting record remains with the meeting owner.

## 9. Keep existing information-management rules in place

Using AI to summarize a meeting does not replace our existing internal information-management rules.

Those rules still apply to recordings and transcripts, including how they are handled and shared. Review should therefore happen within the same approved practices we use for the underlying meeting materials.

The convenience of a generated summary should not lead us to treat the source material differently.

## 10. Takeaway

AI-generated summaries can save time and improve access to meeting outcomes. They are valuable drafts, not automatic records.

Before sharing, the meeting owner should check the facts people will act on: decisions, deadlines, owners, numbers, and open issues. They should also make sure important objections and conditions remain visible.

If we combine AI’s speed with a short, focused human review, we can use the tool confidently without letting fluent wording substitute for accuracy.
