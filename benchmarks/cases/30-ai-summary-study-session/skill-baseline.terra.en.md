# Reviewing AI-Generated Meeting Summaries

## Slide 1 — AI summaries are drafts, not meeting records

Many of us now use AI to turn online-meeting transcripts into summaries. It saves time and can make follow-up easier. But a natural-sounding summary is not automatically an accurate one.

Today’s goal is not to discourage AI use. The goal is to use it safely: treat the output as a helpful draft, then check the facts that matter before sharing it.

By the end of this session, you should know what to review, how to compare it with the meeting record, and who is responsible for the final check.

## Slide 2 — What we found in last month’s trial

We reviewed 50 AI-generated meeting summaries from last month’s trial.

Six contained errors in important facts, such as dates, amounts, or owners. Four attributed statements to the wrong speaker. Five described unresolved matters as if they had already been decided. One summary could contain more than one of these problems.

These numbers do not mean that AI summaries are unusable. They show why they need review. The difficult part is that the prose often sounded fluent and reasonable. Errors can be easy to miss precisely because the summary reads well.

## Slide 3 — The main risk: certainty added where none existed

Consider this example.

The AI summary said: “We will introduce it next month.”

But the actual remark was: “We will consider whether a trial can begin next month.”

The difference is small in wording but large in meaning. The original statement leaves several points open: whether to proceed, whether it is a trial, and whether next month is feasible. The summary turns all of that into a firm decision.

When reviewing, pay particular attention to wording that sounds final: “will,” “approved,” “decided,” “assigned,” or “due.” Check that the original remarks support that level of certainty.

## Slide 4 — Start with the source, not the summary

The transcript is the starting point for review, but it is not perfect evidence by itself. Speech recognition can introduce errors into names, numbers, dates, and technical terms.

So the task is not simply to compare one text against another mechanically. Reviewers should compare the summary with the original remarks and, when something important is unclear, confirm it against the recording or the participants’ shared understanding.

This is especially important when the summary contains information that will cause someone to act: a deadline, a budget amount, an assigned owner, or a decision that affects later work.

## Slide 5 — The five things to check first

When time is limited, review these items first:

- Decisions: Was a decision actually made, or was it still under discussion?
- Deadlines: Are dates, timing, and milestones correct?
- Owners: Is the responsible person or team identified correctly?
- Numbers: Are amounts, quantities, versions, or targets accurate?
- Open issues: Does the summary preserve what remains unresolved?

These checks focus on the parts of a meeting summary most likely to affect work after the meeting. A polished description of background is useful, but it should not take priority over confirming an owner or a deadline.

## Slide 6 — Check what may be missing

Accuracy is not only about correcting statements that are present. It is also about noticing important information that has disappeared.

Ask whether the summary omitted an objection, a condition, or a limitation that changes how a decision should be understood.

For example, “Proceed with the plan” may be misleading if the meeting also included, “provided that Legal approves the revised terms.” Likewise, an action item may appear straightforward while the discussion showed that another team must first provide information.

A concise summary does not need every comment. It does need the conditions and concerns that affect the next action.

## Slide 7 — A practical review sequence

Use this sequence before sharing a summary.

First, read the summary once to understand its overall story.

Next, compare every stated decision, deadline, owner, number, and open issue with the relevant original remarks. Do not assume that a sentence is correct because the surrounding text is accurate.

Then, look for missing objections or conditions, especially around decisions and commitments.

Finally, correct the draft so that it reflects what was actually said. If the meeting did not reach a conclusion, say that clearly. If the transcript is unclear, do not invent certainty; confirm the point with the relevant participant when necessary.

This review does not require rewriting every sentence. It means verifying the statements that readers will rely on.

## Slide 8 — Responsibility before sharing

The person responsible for the meeting reviews the summary before it is shared with participants.

That responsibility remains with the meeting owner even when AI creates the first draft. AI can organize the discussion, but it cannot take responsibility for whether the record is suitable for others to rely on.

After review, record the reviewer’s name and the review date in the document. This lets readers see that the summary was checked and identify who reviewed it if a question arises later.

## Slide 9 — AI use does not replace existing information rules

Existing internal information-management rules still apply to recordings and transcripts.

Using AI to summarize a meeting does not change how we handle the underlying information. Continue to follow the applicable rules for access, storage, sharing, and retention of recordings and transcripts.

The summary may be easier to circulate than the full transcript, but it is still connected to the same meeting and should be handled appropriately.

## Slide 10 — The takeaway

AI-generated summaries can save time and improve follow-up, but they should be used as drafts.

Before sharing, the meeting owner should verify decisions, deadlines, owners, numbers, and open issues against the original remarks. They should also confirm that important objections and conditions have not been omitted, and record who reviewed the summary and when.

The standard is simple: if someone could make a decision or take action based on the summary, the facts behind that action must be checked first.
