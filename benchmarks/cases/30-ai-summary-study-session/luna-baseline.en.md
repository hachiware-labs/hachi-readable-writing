# Reviewing AI-Generated Meeting Summaries

**15-minute internal study session script**

## 0:00–1:00 — Opening

“Today’s session is about reviewing AI-generated meeting summaries.

Many of us now use AI to summarize online meeting transcripts. This can save time and make information easier to share. But an AI summary is still a draft. Before it is distributed, we need to check whether it accurately reflects what was actually said.

The goal today is not to discourage AI use. The goal is to use it safely and confidently.”

---

## 1:00–2:30 — What we found

“During last month’s trial, we reviewed 50 AI-generated summaries.

Six summaries contained errors in important facts, such as dates, amounts, or responsible owners. Four attributed statements to the wrong speaker. Five presented unresolved matters as if they were decisions.

These categories overlap, so we should not add the numbers together as if they represented separate summaries.

The important point is that these errors occurred even though the summaries generally sounded natural and professional. In some cases, the writing was so smooth that the mistakes were easy to overlook.

Natural-sounding prose is not the same as accurate content.”

---

## 2:30–4:00 — Why errors are easy to miss

“AI systems are good at producing clear, organized text. They may turn a complicated discussion into a short list of decisions and actions.

That is useful—but it can also hide uncertainty.

For example, the AI may interpret a tentative suggestion as a commitment. It may assign a comment to the wrong person. It may select a plausible date or amount even when the transcript was unclear.

There is another issue: the transcript itself may contain speech-recognition errors. If a name, number, or technical term was transcribed incorrectly, the AI may build an accurate-looking summary from incorrect source text.

So our review has two layers:

First, does the summary match the transcript?

Second, when the transcript is unclear, does it match the actual meeting remarks or recording?”

---

## 4:00–6:00 — Example: decision versus possibility

“Let’s look at a simple example.

The summary said:

‘We will introduce it next month.’

But the actual remark was:

‘We will consider whether a trial can begin next month.’

These sentences are very different.

The first describes a decision and a commitment. The second describes an unresolved possibility. It also says that the group is considering a trial—not necessarily introducing the finished solution.

This kind of error can create immediate problems. Someone may begin preparing for a launch that was never approved. Participants may believe a deadline has been agreed. Or an external partner may receive information that the meeting never authorized.

When reviewing a summary, pay particular attention to words that indicate certainty:

- decided
- approved
- will
- must
- by Friday
- assigned to

Compare them with words that indicate uncertainty:

- consider
- explore
- may
- could
- subject to
- pending confirmation

AI summaries often make language more definite than the original discussion.”

---

## 6:00–8:30 — The five things to verify

“Our review should focus first on information that can cause practical consequences.

There are five areas to verify.

First, decisions. Was something actually decided, or was it only proposed, discussed, or left for later?

Second, deadlines. Check dates and timeframes against the original remarks. Be especially careful with relative expressions such as ‘next month,’ ‘this Friday,’ or ‘after the release.’

Third, owners. Confirm who is responsible for each action. A speaker who raises an issue is not necessarily the person assigned to resolve it.

Fourth, numbers. Check amounts, quantities, percentages, budgets, and other figures directly against the source. A single digit can change the meaning completely.

Fifth, open issues. Confirm that unresolved questions remain unresolved in the summary. They should not disappear into a list of completed decisions.

A useful review question is:

‘If someone read only this summary, could they take the wrong action?’

If the answer might be yes, that item needs closer checking.”

---

## 8:30–10:30 — Check for omissions

“Reviewing is not only about finding statements that are wrong. We also need to check what is missing.

Important objections, conditions, and limitations may be omitted when the AI compresses a discussion.

For example, someone may say, ‘I support this approach, provided that the security review is completed first.’ If the summary records only ‘The team supported the approach,’ it has removed an important condition.

Similarly, a participant may raise a serious concern about cost, timing, legal review, or operational risk. Even if the group ultimately moves forward, that objection may still be important context.

When reviewing, ask:

- Were major objections included?
- Were conditions and dependencies preserved?
- Were risks or limitations mentioned?
- Were unresolved questions clearly recorded?
- Did the summary leave out a viewpoint that would change how readers understand the discussion?

A short summary does not need to include every sentence. But it should preserve the information needed to understand the decision, the uncertainty, and the next steps.”

---

## 10:30–12:30 — A practical review process

“Here is a practical process that can usually be completed quickly.

Start by reading the summary once to understand its overall message.

Next, review the sections containing decisions, actions, deadlines, owners, and numbers. Compare each important item with the original transcript or meeting record.

Then check speaker attribution. If the identity of the speaker matters, confirm that the statement belongs to the right person. This is particularly important for approvals, commitments, objections, and instructions.

After that, review open issues and look for missing objections or conditions.

Finally, check any part of the transcript that appears uncertain or contains unusual wording. If necessary, listen to the recording. Do not silently guess when the source is ambiguous. Mark the item for confirmation or ask the meeting participants.

A simple review checklist is:

‘Decisions, deadlines, owners, numbers, open issues, objections, and conditions.’

That gives us a consistent minimum standard without requiring us to reread every word of a long meeting.”

---

## 12:30–13:30 — Responsibility and documentation

“The person responsible for the meeting reviews the summary before it is shared with participants.

This responsibility matters because the meeting owner is usually best positioned to recognize the intended meaning of the discussion and identify which points require clarification.

After the review, the document records the reviewer’s name and the review date. This gives readers visible evidence that the summary was checked.

The record does not mean that the summary is guaranteed to be perfect. It tells readers who performed the review and when, so they know the document passed through the expected process.”

---

## 13:30–14:15 — Information-management rules

“Using AI does not replace our existing information-management responsibilities.

The rules that apply to meeting recordings and transcripts still apply. This includes how we handle access, storage, sharing, retention, and other sensitive information.

Before using an AI tool, follow the internal rules for the information involved. After generating a summary, share it only with the appropriate audience.

AI can help us handle information more efficiently, but it does not change the information’s sensitivity or the controls that apply to it.”

---

## 14:15–15:00 — Closing

“To close, AI-generated meeting summaries are valuable when we treat them as drafts rather than authoritative records.

The most important habits are:

Check decisions, deadlines, owners, numbers, and open issues.

Confirm that important objections and conditions were not omitted.

Be alert to speech-recognition errors in the source transcript.

Pay special attention when tentative language has been changed into a definite commitment.

And make sure the meeting owner reviews the summary before it is shared, with the reviewer’s name and review date recorded.

Used this way, AI can reduce the effort of preparing meeting notes while keeping responsibility for accuracy with the people who understand the meeting.

The question is not whether AI can write a good summary. The question is whether the summary is safe for someone else to rely on.”
