# Reviewing AI-Generated Meeting Summaries  
### Internal study session — 15 minutes

## 0:00–1:30 — AI summaries are drafts, not records

Good afternoon. Many of us now use AI to turn online-meeting transcripts into usable summaries. This saves time and makes it easier to share what happened with people who could not attend.

But a summary that reads smoothly can still be wrong. That is the central point of today’s session: use AI to create a strong first draft, then review it before anyone treats it as the meeting record.

The goal is not to discourage AI use. It is to make its use dependable enough for everyday work.

## 1:30–3:30 — What we found in the trial

Last month, we reviewed 50 AI-generated meeting summaries from the trial.

Six contained errors in important facts, such as dates, amounts, or the person responsible for an action. Four attributed statements to the wrong speaker. Five described unresolved matters as though they had already been decided.

These numbers overlap: a single summary could contain more than one type of problem.

The important lesson is that these were not necessarily obviously poor summaries. The prose often sounded natural and confident. That made the mistakes easy to overlook.

## 3:30–5:00 — Why fluent writing can be risky

When a summary is awkward, we tend to check it carefully. When it is concise and well written, we are more likely to assume it is accurate.

AI can also turn tentative discussion into a definite-sounding conclusion. For example, one summary said:

> “We will introduce it next month.”

But the actual remark was:

> “We will consider whether a trial can begin next month.”

Those are very different statements. The first creates a commitment. The second leaves both the decision and the timing open.

A polished sentence is not evidence. The original remarks are.

## 5:00–6:30 — Remember that the transcript is not perfect either

The transcript is our main source for review, but it may also contain speech-recognition errors. Names, numbers, dates, and technical terms can be transcribed incorrectly. Overlapping speech or unclear audio can also change the apparent meaning of a remark.

So the review is not simply a comparison between two texts. It is a check that the summary reflects what was actually said, using the transcript and, where necessary, the recording or the meeting context.

When something important seems unclear, do not guess. Confirm it.

## 6:30–10:30 — What to compare before sharing

The person responsible for the meeting reviews the AI summary before it is shared with participants.

Focus first on the details that affect action, accountability, cost, or expectations:

- Decisions: Was something actually decided, or was it still under discussion?
- Deadlines: Is the date or timing correct, including any conditions attached to it?
- Owners: Is the right person or team assigned to each action?
- Numbers: Are amounts, quantities, budgets, targets, and version numbers accurate?
- Open issues: Are pending questions still shown as open?

Then make a second pass for what may be missing. Check whether the summary left out important objections, risks, alternatives, or conditions.

For instance, a decision may be correct but incomplete if the meeting agreed to proceed only after legal review, only within a budget limit, or only after a trial. Those conditions belong in the summary because they change how people should act.

## 10:30–12:30 — A practical review routine

A simple routine is usually enough.

First, read the AI summary once without editing. Mark every decision, action, date, owner, number, and unresolved issue.

Next, compare each marked item with the original remarks. Start with the transcript, and check the recording or ask a participant if the transcript itself is unclear.

Then correct the wording so that certainty matches the meeting. Replace “will” with “will consider” when that is what was said. Change a named owner if the task was assigned to someone else. Restore an objection or condition if it materially affects the outcome.

Finally, read the completed summary as someone who did not attend the meeting. Would that person understand what was decided, what remains open, who is doing what, and by when? If not, revise before sharing.

## 12:30–13:45 — Make the review visible

After the review, record the reviewer’s name and the review date in the document.

This is not meant to add unnecessary formality. It lets readers see that the summary was checked and identify the person who confirmed it. It also makes the summary easier to rely on when actions are followed up later.

The review record should reflect an actual review. It is not a label to add automatically.

## 13:45–14:30 — Handle source material under existing rules

AI-generated summaries do not change our existing information-management rules.

Recordings and transcripts still need to be handled according to those rules. Continue to apply the same requirements for storage, access, sharing, retention, and any sensitive information in the meeting.

Using AI changes how we create a draft; it does not remove our responsibility for the underlying information.

## 14:30–15:00 — Closing

AI can save us real time, especially when meetings are frequent or detailed. But it is safest when we treat its output as a draft that a responsible person checks before sharing.

Before distribution, compare the important facts and open issues with the original remarks, confirm any unclear transcript content, and record who reviewed the result and when.

That small review step is what turns a fluent AI summary into a reliable meeting summary.
