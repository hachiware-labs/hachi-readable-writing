## Learning to Ride at Her Own Pace

Our daughter began practicing riding a bicycle without training wheels in May, at seven years old.

On the first day, her father held the rear rack as they went around the park. After half a lap, she was frightened and decided to stop. Rather than pushing herself to continue, she listened to how she felt.

The following week, she made her own plan: she would practice for just twenty minutes every Sunday morning. Some Sundays were harder than others. On those days, she made the goal smaller—for example, practicing only turns. Little by little, she found ways to continue without asking too much of herself.

In the middle of June, she fell and scraped her knee. She was shaken and said that she did not want to see the bicycle for the rest of that day. The next week, she still did not ride. Instead, she practiced using the brakes and getting off safely with her mother. It was a quieter kind of practice, but it helped her become comfortable with the bicycle again.

Then, on July 5, she rode about thirty meters along a straight path without realizing that her father had let go. When she stopped and he told her that she had ridden by herself, she looked surprised—and then she laughed.

She still needs a little help when she starts, and she does not yet ride on crowded streets. There is more for her to learn, but we want to remember this stage just as it is.

This was not simply a lesson about never giving up. Sometimes she practiced, sometimes she chose a smaller goal, and sometimes she rested. What mattered was that she learned to notice when she was afraid and to decide for herself what she was ready to try. Watching her find her own pace has been every bit as meaningful as watching her ride on her own.
