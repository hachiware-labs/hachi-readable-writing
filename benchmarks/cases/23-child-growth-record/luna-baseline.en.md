### Learning to Ride

In May, at seven years old, she began practicing riding without training wheels. On the first day, her father held the rear rack as they went around the park. She made it only half a lap before becoming frightened and stopping. That was enough for the day.

Starting the following week, she decided that she would practice for just 20 minutes every Sunday morning. On difficult days, she made the goal smaller. Sometimes practicing only turns felt right; other times, simply getting on and trying was enough.

In mid-June, she fell and scraped her knee. She said she did not want to see the bicycle that day, so she rested. The next week, she still did not ride. Instead, she practiced using the brakes and getting off safely with her mother. Little by little, she found her way back to riding in a manner that felt manageable to her.

Then, on July 5, she rode about 30 meters along a straight path. She did not realize that her father had let go. When she stopped and learned that he was no longer holding the bicycle, she looked surprised—and then laughed.

She still needs a little help getting started, and she does not ride on crowded streets yet. But she has learned more than how to balance on two wheels. She has learned to notice when she is afraid, to rest when she needs to, and to choose a smaller way to practice. Her progress has belonged to her, one Sunday morning at a time.
