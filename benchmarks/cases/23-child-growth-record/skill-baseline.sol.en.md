# Learning to Ride at Her Own Pace

Our daughter began practicing on a bicycle without training wheels in May, when she was seven. On the first day, her father held the rear rack as they set off around the park. After half a lap, she was too frightened to continue. She stopped, and that was enough for the day.

Beginning the following week, she chose a routine that felt manageable: twenty minutes of practice every Sunday morning. She did not always work on riding farther. On difficult days, she made the goal smaller and practiced just one thing, such as turning. Little by little, she found her own way to stay close to the bicycle without asking too much of herself.

In mid-June, she fell and scraped her knee. She was upset and said she did not want to see the bicycle again that day. The next week, she still was not ready to ride. Instead, she practiced using the brakes and getting off safely with her mother. It looked like a quiet week, but she was learning that practice could take different forms—and that she could decide what she was ready to do.

On July 5, she rode about thirty meters along a straight path. Her father had let go of the rear rack, but she did not realize it until she stopped. When he told her that she had ridden by herself, she looked surprised for a moment and then laughed.

She still needs a little help when starting, and she does not yet ride on crowded streets. There is more for her to learn, but we want to remember this part of the journey as it really happened. She did not simply keep going no matter what. She stopped when she was frightened, rested after she was hurt, and made the practice smaller when she needed to. Learning to ride was also learning to notice her own feelings, choose her next step, and move forward at her own pace.
