## Learning to Ride

Our daughter began practicing without training wheels in May, when she was seven. On the first day, her father held the rear rack as she rode around the park. After half a lap, she stopped. The bicycle felt frightening, and that was enough for the day.

From the following week, she chose a rhythm that felt manageable: twenty minutes every Sunday morning. When riding was difficult, she made the goal smaller herself. Some days, instead of trying to go farther, she practiced only turning.

In mid-June, she fell and scraped her knee. That day, she said she did not want to see the bicycle. The next week, she did not ride at all. With her mother beside her, she only practiced using the brakes and getting off safely. It was a quiet return, but it was her way of coming back to it.

On July 5, she rode about thirty meters along a straight path without realizing that her father had let go of the bicycle. When she stopped and learned that he had released it, she looked surprised for a moment, then laughed.

She still needs a little help to start, and she does not ride on crowded streets. But we want to remember more than the distance she rode. She learned that being afraid could mean resting, changing the plan, or practicing one small part at a time—and that she could decide for herself when she was ready to try again.
