## Learning to Ride

In May, at seven years old, she began practicing on a bicycle without training wheels. On the first day, her father held the rear rack as they went around the park. After half a lap, she was frightened and decided to stop.

Beginning the next week, she chose a rhythm that felt manageable: twenty minutes of practice on Sunday mornings. On days when riding felt difficult, she made the goal smaller for herself. Sometimes she practiced only turning.

In mid-June, she fell and scraped her knee. That day, she said she did not want to see the bicycle. The following Sunday, she did not ride at all. Instead, with her mother, she practiced using the brakes and getting off safely.

Then, on July 5, she rode about thirty meters along a straight path. She did not realize at first that her father had let go. When she stopped and learned that she had been riding on her own, she looked surprised—and then laughed.

She still needs a little help getting started, and she does not ride on crowded streets. But we want to remember more than the day she rode alone. She learned to notice when she was afraid, to rest when she needed to, and to decide for herself what kind of practice she was ready to try next.
