## Learning to Ride

In May, at seven years old, she began practicing on a bicycle without training wheels. On the first day, her father held the rear rack as she rode around the park. After half a lap, she stopped. She was frightened, and that was enough for the day.

From the following week, she made her own plan: twenty minutes of practice every Sunday morning. The time was short enough to feel manageable, and she decided what to work on. On days when riding felt difficult, she made the goal smaller. Sometimes she practiced only turning rather than trying to ride farther.

In mid-June, she fell and scraped her knee. That day, she said she did not want to see the bicycle again. We let the bicycle stay where it was. The next week, she did not ride at all. Instead, with her mother beside her, she practiced using the brakes and getting off safely. It was a quiet kind of practice, but it helped her return in her own time.

On July 5, she rode along a straight path for about 30 meters. Her father had been holding the bicycle at first, but at some point he let go. She kept going without noticing. When she stopped and learned that she had been riding by herself, she looked surprised, then laughed.

She still needs a little help to start, and she does not ride on crowded streets. But this summer, she has learned more than how to balance on two wheels. She has learned that being afraid can mean taking a break, that a hard task can be made smaller, and that she can decide when she is ready to try again.
