# Equipment Inventory Prototype — Presentation Script (10 minutes)

## 0:00–0:45 | Opening: the problem we need to solve

Good morning. Today I’d like to introduce a prototype equipment-inventory app and ask for General Affairs’ cooperation in a one-month trial.

At present, equipment at our four sites is managed in separate Excel workbooks. At month end, General Affairs combines those files manually. That takes about six hours every month.

More importantly, the last physical inventory count found 37 differences between the records and the actual equipment. Inconsistent item names and missed updates were contributing factors.

The prototype is intended to make the information easier to update where the equipment is, and easier to trust when we need to count it.

## 0:45–2:00 | What changes with the prototype

The app keeps one equipment record with the essential operational information:

- Asset number
- Item name
- Site
- Storage location
- Condition
- Last verification date

Instead of maintaining separate site files and consolidating them later, staff can update the same record when an item is checked or moved.

For day-to-day inventory work, the key feature is QR-code scanning with a smartphone. A user scans the asset-number QR code, confirms the item, and updates its condition or storage location.

The app also lets users filter records by site and see items that have not yet been verified. This gives the person conducting the count a focused work list rather than a full spreadsheet to sort manually.

## 2:00–2:45 | Accountability and traceability

A record is only useful if we can understand how it changed.

For that reason, the prototype keeps a history of every update: who made it, when they made it, and the values before and after the change.

This does not eliminate the need for careful work, but it makes discrepancies easier to investigate. Instead of asking which spreadsheet was updated most recently, we can look at the update history for the individual item.

## 2:45–5:15 | Live demonstration

I’ll now show the basic inventory-check flow.

[Open the app on the smartphone and display the list filtered to the relevant site and “Not yet verified.”]

First, I have filtered the list to this site and to items that have not yet been verified. This is the list the person conducting the count would work from.

[Show the target item in the unverified list.]

Here is one item waiting for verification.

[Scan its asset-number QR code.]

After scanning the QR code, the app opens the matching equipment record. This avoids searching by a potentially inconsistent item name.

[Show asset number, item name, site, current location, condition, and last verification date.]

The record shows the asset number and the information needed to confirm the item. In this example, the item has been moved, so I will update its storage location.

[Change the storage location and save the update.]

The location is now updated. The verification date is also recorded as part of this check.

[Return to the filtered “Not yet verified” list.]

When we return to the unverified list, this item has disappeared because it has now been verified.

[Open the item’s history.]

Finally, the history shows the person who made the change, the time of the update, and the old and new storage locations.

That is the core workflow: identify the item by QR code, update it at the point of work, and leave an auditable record.

## 5:15–6:30 | Expected value of the trial

The prototype is designed to address the specific issues we see today.

A shared record structure can reduce variation in item names. Updating an item immediately after verification or relocation can reduce missed updates. And filtering by unverified items can make the inventory-count process easier to manage.

We should validate those expectations with actual use rather than assume them. The proposed trial is therefore deliberately limited: one month, approximately 500 items, on the head-office floor.

## 6:30–7:45 | What we will measure

During the trial, we will record four measures:

- Time required to register equipment data
- Time required to conduct the inventory count
- Number of discrepancies between records and physical inventory
- Questions and issues raised by users

These measures will show both the operational benefit and the practical friction. For example, faster counting would be valuable, but only if staff can use the scanning and update process reliably.

At the end of the month, we will compare the results with the current process and use the user questions to identify what must improve before considering a wider rollout.

## 7:45–8:40 | Scope and limitations

I also want to be clear about what this prototype is and is not.

It does not handle purchase requests, depreciation, or integration with the external accounting system. Its purpose is equipment location, condition, and verification management.

It currently contains only 30 sample records, and performance testing has not yet been conducted. The trial with about 500 items is therefore both a usability test and an initial check of how the prototype performs with a more realistic volume.

These limits are why we are asking for a controlled trial, not approval for a full deployment.

## 8:40–10:00 | Request and close

Today, we are asking General Affairs for two forms of support.

First, approval to cooperate in the one-month trial on the head-office floor.

Second, appointment of one person to prepare the equipment data for the approximately 500 trial items. This preparation is necessary so that the trial reflects the real inventory as closely as possible.

In return, we will keep the trial focused, record the four agreed measures, and report the results and the user feedback after the month ends.

The goal is straightforward: determine whether a QR-based shared inventory record can reduce monthly consolidation work and improve the reliability of our physical inventory records.

Thank you.
