# Equipment Inventory App Prototype: Trial Proposal

## 1. Why we built this prototype — 1 minute

Today, each of our four sites manages equipment in a separate Excel workbook. At month end, General Affairs combines those files manually. That work takes about six hours every month.

The current process also makes it harder to keep records aligned with reality. In the last inventory count, we found 37 differences between the records and the physical inventory. Inconsistent item names and updates that were missed or delayed contributed to those differences.

We built this prototype to make everyday updates simpler and to give General Affairs a clearer, more current view of equipment across sites.

## 2. What the prototype does — 1 minute

For each item, the app stores:

- Asset number
- Item name
- Site
- Storage location
- Condition
- Last verification date

The key idea is that the person handling an item can update it where they are. Using a smartphone, they scan the QR code attached to the item’s asset number, then update its condition or location.

The app also lets users filter the inventory by site and see items that have not yet been verified. Every update is recorded with the person who made it, the time of the change, and the values before and after the update.

## 3. What this could improve — 1 minute

This prototype is not intended to replace every administrative system. Its immediate purpose is narrower: make equipment records easier to maintain during normal work and make inventory counts easier to manage.

If location and condition changes are recorded when they happen, General Affairs should spend less time collecting corrections at month end. A shared format can also reduce variation in item names between sites. During inventory counts, the unverified-items list gives staff a direct view of what still needs checking.

The expected benefit is not simply a new screen. It is a more reliable routine: scan, confirm, update, and leave an audit trail.

## 4. Live demonstration — 3 minutes

I will now demonstrate the basic workflow using a sample item.

First, I open the inventory list and filter it to the relevant site. I then select the list of items that have not yet been verified. This view helps an inventory-counting team focus on the remaining work instead of reviewing every item again.

Next, I scan the QR code on the sample item with a smartphone. The app opens the item record using its asset number.

The record shows the item name, current site, storage location, condition, and last verification date. For this demonstration, the item has been moved to a different storage location.

I change the storage location, confirm the item’s condition, and save the update.

Now I return to the unverified-items list. Because this item has just been verified, it no longer appears there.

Finally, I open the item history. The history shows who updated the record, when the update happened, and the previous and new storage locations. This provides a traceable record if General Affairs later needs to confirm why an inventory record changed.

## 5. What the prototype does not yet cover — 1 minute

It is important to be clear about the prototype’s current boundaries.

It does not handle purchase requests, depreciation, or integration with the external accounting system. It contains only 30 sample records, and we have not yet performed performance testing.

For that reason, we are not asking to replace the current management process now. We are asking to test whether this focused workflow is practical with real equipment data and real users.

## 6. Proposed one-month trial — 2 minutes

We propose a one-month trial covering approximately 500 items on the head-office floor.

During the trial, we will measure four things:

- Time required to register equipment data
- Time required to complete the inventory count
- Number of differences between records and physical inventory
- Questions and issues raised by users

These measures will let us compare the practical effort and record quality with the current process. The trial will also show whether the smartphone scanning workflow is convenient enough for daily use and whether the app performs adequately with a larger set of records.

At the end of the month, we will review the results with General Affairs before proposing any broader rollout or additional functions.

## 7. Request to General Affairs — 1 minute

We ask General Affairs to approve cooperation with this one-month trial and appoint one person to prepare the head-office equipment data.

That person would help provide and confirm the initial data for the approximately 500 trial items. We will use the trial to validate the registration process, support the inventory count, and collect feedback from the people using the app.

The decision requested today is limited: approval to run the trial and nomination of a data-preparation contact.

If the trial reduces inventory-count effort, lowers discrepancies, and proves workable for users, we will return with evidence for the next decision.
